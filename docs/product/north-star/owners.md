# Owners

The owner is the one person in the building who can end your career. You don't
pick your owner. You don't control your owner. You manage the relationship — the
same way you manage the relationship with your head coach, except the power
dynamic is reversed. The coach works for you. You work for the owner.

You'll never see a dashboard that says "Owner Patience: 64%." What you'll see is
a phone call after a third straight losing season asking why you haven't fired
the coach yet. You'll see a directive to "make a splash" in free agency when you
know the smart move is to save cap space. You'll see a glowing press conference
after a playoff win that buys you three more years of runway. The owner is a
person with expectations, biases, and a threshold for pain — and your job is to
navigate that reality while building a franchise that wins.

## Design Philosophy

### Owners are opaque

Just like player attributes, scout accuracy, and coach ratings, **you never see
your owner's true personality values**. You don't hire on with a team and see
"Patience: 72, Meddling: 45, Football IQ: 31." You take the job and learn who
your owner is over time — through his reactions, his directives, his public
comments, and how much rope he gives you before he starts pulling it back.

What you know when you take the job:

- **Public reputation**: Is this owner known for patience or for firing GMs
  every two years? Has he built a stable organization, or is this a revolving
  door? This is public record — media coverage, league history, the owner's
  track record with previous GMs.
- **Introductory meeting**: When you take the job, the owner tells you his
  vision. "I want a championship in five years." "Build through the draft, I'm
  willing to be patient." "This franchise hasn't made the playoffs in a decade —
  that changes now." Whether he means it — and whether his patience matches his
  words — is something you'll discover the hard way.
- **Organizational history**: How long did the last GM last? Was he fired after
  a rebuild, or after a failed win-now push? Did the owner meddle in personnel
  decisions, or was the GM given full autonomy? The history tells you what this
  owner values, but history isn't destiny — an owner who fired his last GM for
  losing might be more patient with you if you're winning.

What you never see:

- His true patience threshold
- How much media pressure actually affects him
- Whether he'll follow through on his promises about timeline
- How he'll react when things go wrong

### The owner is not the enemy

The owner system isn't designed to punish the player. It's designed to create
**stakes**. Without an owner, a rebuild has no tension — you can lose for five
years and nobody cares. With an owner, every losing season costs political
capital. Every bold move is a bet with your job on the line. Every playoff run
buys you time. The owner is the clock that makes your decisions matter.

Most of the time, the owner is in the background. You'll go entire seasons
without hearing from him if things are going well. He becomes loud when things
go poorly — or when things go so well that he wants to capitalize.

### No owner is perfect

Every owner has flaws. A patient owner might be _too_ patient — letting a bad GM
run a franchise into the ground for a decade. An impatient owner might fire a
good GM who was two years away from a championship window. A hands-off owner
gives you freedom but might not provide the organizational support you need. A
meddling owner has strong opinions that are sometimes right and often wrong.

The owner you get shapes the kind of GM you have to be. Under a patient owner,
you can execute a five-year rebuild. Under an impatient one, you'd better find
shortcuts — or win fast enough that patience never becomes an issue.

## Owner Personality

Every owner has hidden personality traits that drive their behavior. These are
set when the franchise is generated and evolve slowly (if at all) over the life
of the franchise.

### Patience

The most important trait. How many losing seasons will the owner tolerate before
making changes?

- **High patience**: Willing to endure 3-4 losing seasons as part of a
  communicated rebuild plan. Won't panic after a bad start. Lets the GM execute
  the vision. The danger: a patient owner might let a bad GM destroy the
  franchise because he's too slow to act.
- **Low patience**: Expects immediate competitiveness. A 4-12 season after
  promising "playoffs this year" puts your job in danger. Two straight losing
  seasons and the owner is interviewing replacements. The danger: an impatient
  owner creates a revolving door that prevents any GM from building something
  lasting.

Patience is not static. It erodes with losing and regenerates with winning. An
owner who started patient can become impatient after four straight losing
seasons. An impatient owner who watches you build a dynasty becomes more
trusting over time. Patience is a reservoir — wins fill it, losses drain it, and
you never see the water level.

### Meddling

How much the owner involves himself in football decisions.

- **Hands-off**: Hires a GM and lets him work. Doesn't push for specific
  players, doesn't demand coaching changes, doesn't call after a bad loss. The
  GM has full autonomy — which means full accountability.
- **Meddling**: Has opinions about everything. Wants to know why you didn't
  draft the local kid. Thinks the coach should throw the ball more. Calls after
  losses to "discuss direction." Pushes for flashy free agent signings because
  he wants the team on TV. A meddling owner's input is sometimes good (he might
  see something you don't), usually bad (he's not a football guy), and always a
  factor you have to manage.
- **The spectrum**: Most owners fall between the extremes. A mostly hands-off
  owner might become meddling during a losing streak. A meddling owner might
  back off after you win a championship. The trait sets the baseline, but
  context shifts the behavior.

### Football knowledge

How well the owner actually understands the game.

- **High football IQ**: His opinions about players and coaches are sometimes
  genuinely insightful. When he pushes back on a decision, it's worth
  considering whether he has a point. A knowledgeable meddling owner is
  manageable — frustrating, but his interventions occasionally help.
- **Low football IQ**: His opinions are driven by media narratives, fan
  sentiment, and gut feelings. He thinks the team needs a quarterback because
  ESPN told him so, not because he's evaluated the roster. A low-IQ meddling
  owner is a disaster — he pushes for bad decisions with the authority to make
  you listen.
- **Football knowledge and meddling interact**: A hands-off owner's football IQ
  is irrelevant — he's not using it. A meddling owner's football IQ determines
  whether his involvement helps or hurts.

### Spending willingness

How much the owner is willing to invest in the non-cap areas of the
organization.

- **Big spender**: Invests in scouting infrastructure, analytics departments,
  training facilities, medical staff, and front office support. These
  investments improve your scouting operation, player development environment,
  and overall organizational quality. A big-spending owner gives you better
  tools to work with.
- **Frugal**: Keeps non-cap spending low. Your scouting department is
  understaffed. Your analytics tools are outdated. Your training facilities are
  mediocre. You can still win — but you're operating with a structural
  disadvantage that other franchises don't have.
- **The cap is the cap**: Owner spending willingness does NOT affect the salary
  cap. Every team has the same cap. What varies is the organizational
  infrastructure around it — and that's where owner spending makes a difference.

### Public profile

How the owner handles the public-facing side of the franchise.

- **Low-profile**: Stays out of the media. Lets the GM and coach be the faces of
  the organization. Doesn't do press conferences unless required. This insulates
  you from ownership-driven media narratives but also means the owner won't
  publicly back you when the heat is on.
- **High-profile**: Loves the spotlight. Does interviews, makes public
  statements about the team's direction, and inserts himself into narratives.
  This can help (a public endorsement from the owner buys you time) or hurt (a
  public critique from the owner signals your days are numbered). High-profile
  owners amplify everything — praise and criticism alike.

## Owner Directives

Owners don't set your lineup or make your picks. But they do set expectations,
and those expectations shape the context you operate in.

### How directives work

Periodically — typically after the season, but sometimes mid-season during a
crisis — the owner communicates his expectations. These aren't orders you must
follow. They're the lens through which the owner evaluates your performance.

- **"Win now"**: The owner expects the team to compete this season. He's
  watching the win-loss record and expects postseason contention. A
  rebuild-oriented GM who hears "win now" has a problem — either adjust the
  timeline or convince the owner that patience will pay off. How persuasive you
  can be depends on your track record and the owner's personality.
- **"I trust the process"**: The owner is granting runway for a rebuild. He'll
  tolerate losing if he sees progress — young players developing, draft capital
  accumulating, a visible plan taking shape. But this isn't infinite patience.
  "Trust the process" for two years becomes "where are the results?" by year
  four.
- **"Make a splash"**: The owner wants a marquee move — a blockbuster trade, a
  big free agent signing, something that generates excitement and sells tickets.
  This is the meddling owner's signature directive. The move he wants might not
  be the move you'd make. You can comply, resist, or find a middle ground — but
  ignoring it has relationship consequences.
- **"Fix the coaching"**: The owner has lost confidence in the coaching staff.
  He may not demand a firing, but the implication is clear — if results don't
  improve, he expects changes. This puts you in a difficult position if you
  believe in your coaches.
- **"Develop the young quarterback"**: A specific personnel directive. The owner
  has decided the franchise QB is on the roster and expects the organization to
  build around him. Whether you agree is your problem.

### Responding to directives

You always have a choice:

- **Comply**: Do what the owner wants. This maintains the relationship, even if
  the football decision is suboptimal. Sometimes the owner is right, and
  compliance produces good results. Sometimes he's wrong, and you've compromised
  your vision to keep your job.
- **Partially comply**: Find a middle ground. The owner says "make a splash" and
  you make a solid-but-not-spectacular trade that addresses a real need. It's
  not the blockbuster he wanted, but it's defensible. Partial compliance buys
  some goodwill without fully sacrificing your strategy.
- **Ignore**: Do what you think is right regardless of the directive. This works
  if you win. If you ignore the owner and then lose, you've burned political
  capital and given him a reason to question your judgment. High- patience,
  hands-off owners tolerate being ignored. Low-patience, meddling owners do not.

The owner never forces your hand. He can't make you trade for a player or fire
your coach. But the consequences of defiance accumulate over time. An owner who
feels ignored stops trusting his GM — and a GM without his owner's trust is on
borrowed time.

## Owner Patience System

Patience is the currency of the GM-owner relationship. Every decision you make,
every season that plays out, every media cycle — they all deposit into or
withdraw from a patience account you can't see.

### What builds patience

- **Winning seasons**: The simplest way to keep your owner happy. Winning solves
  almost everything.
- **Playoff appearances**: Worth more than regular season wins. A playoff team
  with 10 wins builds more patience than a non-playoff team with 10 wins.
- **Championships**: The ultimate patience builder. A championship buys years of
  runway — even the most impatient owner gives a champion time.
- **Visible progress**: During a rebuild, patience comes from showing the plan
  is working. A young roster that improves from 4-13 to 7-10 to 10-7 builds
  patience even before the playoffs. But the trajectory has to be upward —
  stagnation is as damaging as regression.
- **Good press**: Positive media coverage builds patience indirectly. An owner
  who reads that his GM's draft class was "the best in the league" feels good
  about the direction. See [Media](./media.md) for how coverage affects
  perception.
- **Compliance with directives**: An owner who feels heard and respected
  maintains trust, even through rough patches. Listening doesn't mean obeying —
  but acknowledging the owner's perspective matters.

### What drains patience

- **Losing seasons**: The primary drain. How much depends on the owner's
  patience trait and whether losses were expected (rebuild year) or unexpected
  (a contender collapsing).
- **Regression**: Worse than losing. A team that goes from 10-7 to 6-11 drains
  patience faster than a rebuilding team that goes from 3-14 to 4-13. Regression
  signals that the plan isn't working.
- **Failed big moves**: A blockbuster trade that backfires, a high-profile free
  agent who busts, a first-round pick who flames out — these hurt more than
  their football impact because they're visible, public failures that the owner,
  the media, and the fans all see.
- **Negative media coverage**: Sustained criticism erodes the owner's
  confidence. "Every analyst says we're the worst-run franchise in the league"
  is hard for any owner to ignore, even a patient one.
- **Ignoring directives**: Defying the owner costs patience proportional to the
  owner's meddling trait. A hands-off owner barely notices. A meddling owner
  feels disrespected.
- **Coaching carousel**: Firing coaches frequently, especially without improved
  results, signals instability. The owner starts wondering if the problem is the
  coaches — or the GM who keeps hiring and firing them.

### What happens when patience runs out

When the owner's patience is exhausted, the consequences escalate:

1. **Increased pressure**: The owner becomes more vocal. Directives become more
   specific and more insistent. You feel the walls closing in. This is a warning
   — change course or produce results.
2. **Public criticism**: A high-profile owner may make public statements that
   undermine your authority. "We expected to be further along by now" in a press
   conference is the owner telling the world he's losing faith. This affects
   free agent attractiveness and coaching candidate interest — why join a
   franchise where the GM might be gone next year?
3. **Restricted autonomy**: The owner starts vetoing moves. A trade you want to
   make gets blocked. A coaching hire gets overridden. You're still the GM in
   title, but the owner is making the decisions. This is the death spiral — a GM
   without autonomy can't execute a coherent strategy.
4. **Firing**: The final consequence. You're fired.

### Getting fired

Getting fired is not game over. It's a career event — painful, public, and
consequential, but not fatal.

When you're fired:

- **Your current franchise continues without you** — an NPC GM takes over. Your
  roster, your cap situation, your draft picks — they're someone else's problem
  now. You'll watch from the outside as your successor either builds on what you
  started or tears it down.
- **You enter the GM hiring market** — other teams with openings may interview
  you. Your track record follows you: win-loss record, playoff appearances,
  notable transactions, and how the media covered your tenure. A GM fired after
  a championship window closed gets better offers than one fired for
  incompetence.
- **You take a new job** — when you're hired by another team, you inherit their
  roster, their coaches, their cap situation, and their owner. A new owner with
  a new personality, a new set of expectations, and a new patience reservoir.
  The cycle begins again.
- **Or you don't** — if no team offers you a job, you can wait a year and
  re-enter the market (like a coach sitting out). Your reputation may recover —
  or it may not. A GM who was fired for losing might look better after his
  successor does worse.

Getting fired and rebuilding elsewhere is one of the most compelling long- term
narratives the game can produce. The GM who was fired by the Cowboys, hired by
the Jaguars, and built a dynasty there — that's a story worth playing through.

## NPC Owners

Every team in the league has an owner, including NPC teams. NPC owner
personalities affect how NPC GMs operate.

### How NPC owners shape NPC behavior

- An impatient NPC owner pressures his NPC GM to win now — leading to aggressive
  trades, big free agent signings, and shorter coaching tenures. These are the
  NPC teams that create deadline trade frenzy and overpay in free agency.
- A patient NPC owner lets his NPC GM execute long-term plans — leading to
  steady rebuilds, developmental drafting, and coaching stability. These are the
  NPC teams that quietly become contenders after years of patience.
- A meddling NPC owner with low football IQ produces the most entertaining
  dysfunction — bad coaching hires, panic trades, and a franchise that can't get
  out of its own way. These are the league's punching bags, and their
  dysfunction creates opportunities for smart GMs to exploit (trade targets, cap
  casualties, free agents who want out).
- A big-spending NPC owner with a competent NPC GM is the scariest opponent — a
  well-run franchise with organizational advantages that compound over years.

### Owner stability

Owners are long-tenured. Unlike GMs and coaches, owners don't get fired.
Ownership changes are rare, dramatic events:

- **Ownership sale**: An owner sells the franchise. The new owner arrives with a
  new personality and new expectations. This can be a fresh start for a
  dysfunctional franchise — or the beginning of a new kind of dysfunction.
  Ownership sales happen rarely and should feel like significant league events.
- **Generational transfer**: An aging owner hands the franchise to a son,
  daughter, or family member. The new owner may share the previous owner's
  values — or may be completely different. The kid who grew up watching his
  father run the team patiently might be an impatient, meddling owner who wants
  to put his stamp on things immediately.

When ownership changes, the GM's patience reservoir resets. The new owner
evaluates you fresh — your track record matters, but the relationship starts
from zero. A GM who was on thin ice under the old owner might get new life under
the new one. Or the new owner might clean house on day one.

## Interaction with Other Systems

### Media

Media coverage is the primary channel through which the owner forms opinions.
See [Media](./media.md) for the full media system. The key interactions:

- Positive coverage builds patience; negative coverage erodes it
- A high-profile owner amplifies media narratives — his public statements become
  media events themselves
- Media pressure on coaching changes often reflects the owner's private feelings
  — when analysts start calling for the coach's head, the owner may already be
  thinking the same thing
- The owner's public statements affect free agent attractiveness and coaching
  candidate interest (see [Free Agency](./free-agency-and-contracts.md) and
  [Coaches](./coaches.md))

### Coaches

The owner's expectations create indirect pressure on coaching decisions:

- An impatient owner makes it harder to keep a coach through a rough patch —
  even if the coach is developing young players, the owner sees losses
- A meddling owner may push for specific coaching hires, especially high-profile
  candidates who generate buzz
- Coaching buyouts come out of the salary cap, which means an owner who pushes
  you to fire a coach is pushing you to take a cap hit
- A patient owner who backs his coach gives the entire organization stability —
  players know the scheme won't change, scouts know what to draft for, and the
  coaching staff develops without fear

### Salary Cap

Owner spending willingness affects the organizational infrastructure around the
cap, not the cap itself:

- Scouting department budget (see [Scouting](./scouting.md))
- Analytics tools and front office support staff
- Training facilities and medical staff quality
- These investments create structural advantages that compound over years — a GM
  working for a big-spending owner has a better scouting operation, a better
  development environment, and better tools for evaluation

### Free Agency

The owner's public profile and organizational reputation affect free agent
attractiveness:

- An owner known for stability and winning attracts free agents
- An owner known for dysfunction and coaching carousels deters them
- A meddling owner who publicly criticizes players sends a message that free
  agents hear — even if you, the GM, run a professional operation

### The Draft

Meddling owners occasionally push for specific draft picks:

- "The fans want us to take the local quarterback" — a directive that may not
  align with your board
- "We need a playmaker, not another lineman" — the owner overvaluing flashy
  positions, a common real-world dynamic
- Ignoring these preferences costs patience but is sometimes the right football
  decision

## What Makes Owners Fun

The owner system is fun because it adds **stakes to every season** and creates
the kind of pressure that makes good decisions feel earned and bad decisions
feel costly.

The best owner moments:

- "My owner told me to win now, but I know we're two years away. Do I mortgage
  the future to keep my job, or do I bet that the rebuild will be so impressive
  he gives me time?"
- "I just won the championship and my owner is ecstatic. I have at least three
  years of runway — time to reload without pressure. This is the moment I've
  been building toward."
- "My owner is a meddler with terrible football instincts. He wants me to trade
  up for the flashy QB. My scouts say the offensive tackle is the pick. If I
  take the tackle and he busts, I'm fired. If I take the QB to appease the owner
  and he busts, at least I followed the directive. What's the right play?"
- "I got fired after three straight losing seasons. The rebuild was working — my
  young core was about to break through. Now I'm watching an NPC GM trade away
  the players I developed. Three years from now, the franchise I built will win
  a championship under someone else's name. Or it'll collapse. Either way, it
  stings."
- "I took the Jacksonville job after getting fired. Terrible roster, no cap
  space, frugal owner, but he's patient. Four years later, we're in the
  championship game. This is redemption."
- "The franchise just got sold. New owner, new expectations. He's impatient and
  wants to win immediately. My careful rebuild just got a new timeline. Do I
  accelerate, or do I stay the course and risk getting fired by a guy who
  doesn't understand what I've built?"
