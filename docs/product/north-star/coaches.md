# Coaches

Coaches are the most important hires you'll make as a GM — and the ones you
control the least once they're in the building. A coach isn't a set of sliders
you tune. He's a person with a philosophy, biases, strengths, blind spots, and
an ego. You hire him, you give him a roster, and then you watch what he does
with it. Sometimes you're thrilled. Sometimes you're reaching for the phone to
call his replacement.

You don't set the scheme. You don't set the depth chart. You don't call the
plays. Your coaches do. Your job is to put the right coaches in the building,
give them the players they need, and make the hard call when it's not working.
That's it. That's the job.

## Design Philosophy

### Coaches are opaque

Just like player attributes and scout accuracy, **you never see a coach's true
ratings**. You don't hire an offensive coordinator and see "Play Calling: 78,
Player Development: 65, Run Game: 82." You hire a person with a resume, a
reputation, an interview, and a track record you can study if he's been in the
league long enough.

What you know when hiring:

- **Resume**: Where has he coached? What roles? How long? Did his teams win? Did
  his units perform? This is public record, but it doesn't tell you _why_ things
  went well or poorly — was it the coach, or was it the talent?
- **Reputation**: What do other coaches and media say about him? "Offensive
  innovator," "players' coach," "old school disciplinarian" — these labels carry
  signal but also noise.
- **Interview**: When you interview a coaching candidate, you get subjective
  impressions — his vision, his scheme preferences, his philosophy on player
  development. Whether he's right about himself is another question entirely.
- **Track record with you**: If he's been on your staff, you have years of data
  on how his units performed, how his players developed, and how he handled
  adversity. This is the most valuable information and takes years to
  accumulate.
- **Coaching tree**: Who mentored him? Which system did he come up in? A coach
  from the Shanahan tree probably runs outside zone. A coach from the Belichick
  tree probably preaches versatility and discipline. The tree tells you about
  his DNA — but not every branch produces the same fruit.

What you never see:

- His true ability ratings in any category
- His hidden potential ceiling
- His exact biases
- How he'll respond to adversity, losing, or a directive he disagrees with

You learn all of this the same way a real GM does: by watching, over years, and
drawing your own conclusions.

### The game can pass a coach by

This is the Jon Gruden problem. A coach who was elite in 2002 returns in 2018
and the game has changed. The spread revolution happened. RPOs happened. The
rules changed. His mental model of football is fifteen years out of date, and
his hidden attributes haven't kept up with where the league has moved.

Under the hood, the simulation evolves. Offensive and defensive meta-strategies
shift over time — new scheme innovations emerge, certain approaches become more
or less effective as the league adapts. A coach whose knowledge and adaptability
haven't kept pace will run schemes that are increasingly exploitable. His
play-calling patterns become predictable. His development techniques fall behind
modern methods.

This means:

- **A coaching hire is time-sensitive.** A coach who's perfect for your team
  today might not be in five years — not because he got worse, but because the
  league got better around him.
- **Retreads are risky.** A fired coach who sits out for three years and gets
  rehired may have stagnated while out of the game. Or he may have used the time
  to study and adapt. You won't know until he's back on a sideline.
- **Young coaches are bets on trajectory.** A 35-year-old QB coach with modern
  ideas and high hidden potential might become a top-5 play-caller in three
  years. Or he might plateau. The upside is real — but so is the risk.
- **Veteran coaches offer certainty at the cost of ceiling.** A 58-year-old
  coordinator with a 20-year track record is a known quantity. You know what
  you're getting. But "what you're getting" may be a system that opposing
  coaches have long since figured out.

### No overall rating

There is no OVR for coaches. Not for the user, and not under the hood. The
simulation evaluates coaches across dozens of specific attributes in specific
contexts — just like players. A coordinator's value isn't a single number. It's
the interaction between his scheme knowledge, his play-calling tendencies, his
ability to develop specific position groups, his game-day adjustments, his
relationship with players, and a dozen other factors.

What users see instead:

- **Team and unit performance** — your offense ranked 3rd in scoring, your
  defense ranked 22nd in yards allowed, your special teams gave up two return
  touchdowns. These are the report card.
- **Player development trajectories** — your 2nd-round CB improved dramatically
  under Coach X. Your 1st-round QB stagnated under Coach Y. Is it the coach, the
  player, or the scheme? You decide.
- **Depth chart decisions** — your DC is starting the veteran over the talented
  rookie. Is he right? Is he stubborn? Is he protecting his job by playing the
  safe option? You'll find out when the games are played.
- **Scheme tendencies** — you can see what your coaches call. Run-pass ratios,
  blitz frequency, formation tendencies, personnel lean (% 11 vs. 12 vs. 21 on
  offense; % nickel vs. base vs. dime on defense), motion rate, disguise rate,
  and situational splits (red zone, short yardage, third-down-by-distance,
  two-minute). You can't see _why_ they make those calls, but you can evaluate
  whether the results are working. See
  [Schemes & Strategy](./schemes-and-strategy.md) for the full vocabulary of
  personnel groupings, positional labels, pressure packages, and situational
  tendencies these numbers describe.

## Staff Structure

Your coaching staff mirrors a real NFL organization. Every role matters, and
every hire is a decision with cascading consequences.

### Head Coach

The head coach is the leader of the entire coaching staff and the face of your
football operation. But head coaches are not all the same — their role depends
on their personality and background.

**Play-calling head coaches** call plays on their side of the ball (offense or
defense, depending on their background) and defer to the coordinator on the
other side. A head coach who came up as an offensive coordinator will likely
call offensive plays himself, making the OC role more of an assistant/game
planner. These coaches are deeply involved in scheme and execution on their side
but may have blind spots on the other side of the ball.

**CEO-type head coaches** delegate play-calling to both coordinators and focus
on the big picture: team culture, game-day management (clock, challenges, 4th
down decisions), staff cohesion, and managing the locker room. These coaches
live or die by the coordinators they hire — a CEO-type HC with a bad OC is in
trouble because he can't step in and fix it himself.

Which type a coach is depends on his hidden personality traits. You'll get
signals in the interview — "I'm a hands-on offensive guy" vs. "I believe in
empowering my coordinators" — but whether he's actually good at the thing he
claims is another matter.

**Head coach responsibilities:**

- Sets the overall team culture and philosophy
- Game-day management (clock, timeouts, challenges, 4th down decisions)
- May call plays on one side of the ball (personality-dependent)
- Hires and manages the coaching staff (with your approval on major hires)
- Manages locker room dynamics and player relationships
- Handles media (affects team morale and public perception)
- Provides input on roster decisions (he'll tell you what he needs — whether you
  listen is your call)

### Coordinators

**Offensive Coordinator (OC):**

- Designs and installs the offensive scheme
- Calls offensive plays (unless the HC calls them, in which case the OC is the
  primary game planner and assistant)
- Manages the offensive position coaches
- Directly affects offensive performance and player development on that side

**Defensive Coordinator (DC):**

- Designs and installs the defensive scheme
- Calls defensive plays (unless the HC calls them)
- Manages the defensive position coaches
- Directly affects defensive performance and player development on that side

**Special Teams Coordinator (STC):**

- Manages all special teams units
- Often overlooked — but a bad STC costs you field position, and field position
  wins games
- Responsible for identifying special teams contributors on the roster

### Position Coaches

This is where development happens. Position coaches are the most direct
influence on whether your players improve, stagnate, or regress. Every position
group has a dedicated coach:

- **QB Coach** — works daily with quarterbacks on mechanics, reads, and
  decision-making
- **Running Backs Coach** — ball security, vision, pass protection technique
- **Wide Receivers Coach** — route running, releases, hands technique
- **Tight Ends Coach** — the dual-role challenge: blocking technique AND
  receiving development
- **Offensive Line Coach** — technique, communication, combo blocking; often the
  most important position coach on staff
- **Defensive Line Coach** — pass rush moves, gap discipline, hand technique
- **Linebackers Coach** — tackling, coverage drops, blitz execution
- **Defensive Backs Coach** — coverage technique, ball skills, tackling in space
- **Special Teams Assistant** — works under the STC on unit-specific coaching

**Why position coaches matter:**

A great position coach can be the difference between a 1st-round pick developing
into a franchise player and busting out of the league. Under the hood, the
position coach's hidden development ability directly multiplies (or diminishes)
the development rate of every player in his group. A QB coach with elite
development ability and a young quarterback with high hidden potential is a
combination that produces Pro Bowlers. The same quarterback under a mediocre QB
coach might never reach his ceiling.

You'll never see this directly. What you'll see: "My rookie QB improved
significantly this offseason" or "My 2nd-year WR seems to have stagnated." Was
it the player? The coach? The scheme fit? The reps? You'll have to figure it out
by watching patterns over time — the same way you learn your scouts.

## Hidden Attributes

Every coach has attributes across several categories that drive their
effectiveness. All of these are hidden from the user.

### Scheme Knowledge

- **Offensive/Defensive system mastery** — how deeply the coach understands his
  primary scheme; a coordinator with deep mastery runs a more effective, more
  adaptable version of his system
- **Scheme breadth** — how many systems the coach can competently run; a
  one-scheme coach is predictable but may be elite at that one thing; a
  versatile coach can adapt but may master none
- **Innovation** — ability to create new wrinkles, adapt to trends, and stay
  ahead of the league's tactical evolution; high-innovation coaches are harder
  to game-plan against and age better as the meta shifts
- **Adaptability** — how quickly a coach adjusts his scheme when it's not
  working, both within a game (halftime adjustments) and across a season
- **Situational tendency coherence** — whether the coach's red zone, short
  yardage, two-minute, and third-down packages reflect a unified identity or
  feel like a different playbook in each situation; high coherence means
  opposing coordinators see a consistent scheme; low coherence means the coach
  has seams in specific situations that film study will find

### Player Development

- **Technical development** — ability to improve players' technique and skill
  attributes; the core "coaching up" ability
- **Physical development** — ability to design training programs that maximize
  players' physical tools without increasing injury risk
- **Mental development** — ability to improve players' football IQ, decision-
  making, composure, and clutch; the "teaching" dimension
- **Youth development** — specific aptitude for developing young, raw players;
  some coaches are great with veterans but can't develop rookies, and vice versa
- **Positional expertise** — a coach's development ability varies by position
  group, tied to his career background; a former DB coach turned DC will still
  have elite DB development ability even as a coordinator

### Game-Day Management

- **Play-calling** — quality of in-game play selection; reading the opponent's
  tendencies and exploiting weaknesses in real-time
- **Halftime adjustments** — ability to identify what's not working and fix it;
  the best coaches win the second half
- **Clock management** — timeout usage, end-of-half/game strategy, pace of play
  decisions
- **Situational decision-making** — 4th down calls, 2-point conversion
  decisions, when to be aggressive vs. conservative
- **In-game adaptability** — how quickly a coach changes the plan when the
  original approach isn't working

### Evaluation and Personnel

- **Player evaluation** — how accurately a coach assesses his own players'
  abilities; this drives depth chart decisions, and an inaccurate evaluator will
  start the wrong players
- **Scheme fit assessment** — ability to identify which players fit his system
  and which don't; a coach with poor scheme fit assessment might try to force a
  square peg into a round hole
- **Roster input quality** — when the coach tells you "I need a 3-technique DT"
  or "this QB isn't the guy," how reliable is that assessment? Some coaches are
  excellent talent evaluators; others are terrible and will lead you astray if
  you listen to them

### Personality and Leadership

- **Motivational ability** — impact on player morale and effort; some coaches
  get the most out of average talent through sheer force of personality
- **Locker room management** — ability to manage egos, resolve conflicts, and
  maintain team cohesion
- **Discipline** — how well the coach enforces accountability; low-discipline
  coaches have teams that commit penalties and make mental errors
- **Stubbornness** — how resistant the coach is to outside input, including your
  directives as GM; a stubborn coach may ignore your "play the rookies"
  directive because he believes in his veterans; stubbornness isn't always bad —
  a coach who sticks to his system through a rough patch might be vindicated
- **Media handling** — how the coach manages public pressure; a coach who melts
  down in press conferences affects team morale differently than one who
  deflects and stays steady
- **Ego** — a coach with a big ego might clash with a strong-personality
  quarterback or resist your roster suggestions; but ego also drives
  competitiveness and refusal to accept losing

### Biases

Every coach has biases that systematically affect their decisions. These are
hidden and discovered over time:

- **Attribute biases** — a coach who overvalues speed might start a fast but raw
  player over a polished but slow one; a coach who overvalues experience might
  bury a talented rookie behind a declining veteran
- **Scheme biases** — a coach who came up in a certain system will favor players
  who fit that system, even when the roster suggests a different approach
- **Loyalty biases** — some coaches are loyal to "their guys" — players they
  drafted, developed, or brought with them from a previous stop; loyalty can be
  a strength (stability, trust) or a weakness (inability to move on from a
  declining player)
- **Positional biases** — a coach with a DB background might over-invest in the
  secondary during the draft and undervalue the offensive line
- **Personnel biases** — some OCs stay in 11 personnel on 75% of snaps even when
  12 personnel would exploit a light-box defense; some DCs refuse to leave
  nickel even against 22-personnel heavy looks. Personnel rigidity is a form of
  bias — the coach's comfort with his preferred grouping outweighs the matchup
  advantage a different grouping would create
- **Risk tolerance** — some coaches are aggressive (4th down attempts, deep
  shots, blitzing); others are conservative (punt on 4th and 3, run the ball
  with a lead, play soft coverage); neither is inherently right, but mismatches
  between risk tolerance and roster construction are costly

## Coaching Trees and Connections

Coaches don't exist in a vacuum. They come from coaching trees — lineages of
mentorship and shared philosophy that shape how they think about football.

### How trees work

Every coach has a history: who mentored him, which staffs he served on, which
systems he learned. This background shapes his scheme preferences, development
philosophy, and coaching style. A coach who spent five years as a QB coach under
an elite offensive mind will carry elements of that system into his own career.

Coaching trees create tendencies, not determinism. Two coaches from the same
tree will share a foundation — similar scheme language, similar practice
structure, similar evaluation criteria — but they'll diverge based on their own
experiences, hidden attributes, and the staffs they've built since.

### Connections and networking

Coaches have professional networks built from their career history. These
networks matter:

- **Hiring pipeline**: When you need to fill a position coach role, your head
  coach and coordinators will have connections — former colleagues, former
  assistants, coaches they've worked with on other staffs. These connections can
  surface candidates you wouldn't otherwise find, and a connected coach is more
  likely to attract quality assistants.
- **Staff cohesion**: Coaches who share a tree or have worked together before
  have built-in familiarity. They share scheme language, communication styles,
  and coaching philosophy. This reduces installation time for a new system and
  improves coordination between units.
- **Poaching risk**: A rising-star coordinator on your staff has connections
  across the league. When a head coaching job opens, his network is what gets
  him interviews. A well-connected assistant is more likely to be poached —
  which means your coaching staff's success can be self-undermining. You develop
  a great OC, he gets noticed, and now you're starting over.
- **Interview access**: Your own network (and your existing coaches' networks)
  affects which candidates are willing to interview with your organization. A
  team with a reputation for developing coaches attracts better candidates. A
  team that fires coaches every two years might struggle to land top talent.

### The staff-building puzzle

Coaching trees create a team-building dimension within team-building:

- Do you hire from one tree for maximum cohesion, or mix trees for diverse
  perspectives?
- A HC and OC from the same tree will be aligned on scheme — but if one gets
  fired, you might lose the whole offensive philosophy
- A DC from a different tree than the HC might bring a fresh defensive approach
  — or might clash philosophically
- Position coaches from the HC's tree integrate faster but might create an echo
  chamber

## Progression and Regression

Coaches are living careers, not static hires. They grow, peak, plateau, and
decline — just like the players and scouts they work alongside.

### Career arcs

**The rising star:**

A young position coach or coordinator whose hidden attributes are still
developing. His scheme knowledge is growing with every season. His player
development skills are sharpening. He's making mistakes — bad game-day calls,
questionable depth chart decisions — but he's learning from them. In three
years, he might be the best coordinator in the league. Or he might hit his
hidden potential ceiling and plateau as a solid-but-unspectacular coach.

**The established veteran:**

A coach in his prime. He's developed his system, knows his strengths, and has a
track record you can evaluate. His hidden attributes are near or at their
ceiling. What you see is what you get — which is valuable because predictability
reduces risk. The danger: the game is evolving around him, and his innovation
attribute determines whether he evolves with it.

**The retread:**

A coach who was fired and is looking for a second (or third, or fourth) chance.
The question is always: why was he fired? If it was a talent problem (bad
roster, no QB), he might be the same good coach he always was. If the game
passed him by, time away from coaching has only made it worse. If he used the
time away to study and adapt, he might come back sharper.

The retread is one of the highest-variance hires in the game. Some retreads are
Jeff Fisher — a coach who was once good, returned to a league that had changed,
and failed because his approach no longer worked. Others are Bill Belichick —
fired by Cleveland, studied the game obsessively, returned as perhaps the
greatest coach of all time. The hidden attributes tell the story, but you'll
never see them.

**The declining legend:**

A coach who was genuinely elite but is losing his edge. His scheme knowledge is
outdated. His innovation has slowed. His adaptability — never his strongest
trait — can't keep up with the league's evolution. But his reputation is
enormous, and his resume is Hall of Fame caliber. Hiring him feels safe. It
isn't.

The cruelest version: a coach whose player development skills are still strong
but whose game-day management has declined. Your players love him. They're
getting better. But you're losing games because his play-calling is predictable
and his halftime adjustments are nonexistent. Do you fire a beloved coach who's
developing your roster? There's no clean answer.

### Development factors

What determines how a coach grows:

- **Hidden potential** — just like players, every coach has a ceiling for each
  attribute; some have the capacity to become all-time greats, others will max
  out as competent position coaches
- **Experience** — coaches improve with reps; a first-time coordinator learns
  from his mistakes faster than his successes
- **Environment** — a position coach working under an elite coordinator develops
  faster; a coordinator working under a great HC develops faster; coaching
  development cascades down the staff
- **League evolution** — a coach's innovation and adaptability attributes
  determine whether he keeps up with the league's tactical shifts; coaches with
  low innovation stagnate even if their raw coaching ability is high
- **Success and failure** — some coaches respond to adversity by adapting
  (composure, self-awareness); others become more rigid (stubbornness, ego); how
  a coach responds to a losing season is itself a hidden trait

### Aging and the game passing you by

The most important coaching dynamic in the simulation: **the league evolves, and
not every coach evolves with it.**

Under the hood, the game's tactical meta shifts over time. New offensive
concepts emerge. Defensive counter-strategies develop. Rules changes alter
what's effective. A coach's relevance depends on whether his scheme knowledge,
innovation, and adaptability keep pace.

- **High innovation + high adaptability**: This coach evolves. His system in
  year 10 looks different from year 1 because he's incorporated new ideas while
  keeping his core principles. He ages gracefully.
- **High mastery + low innovation**: This coach is elite at running _his_
  system, but his system is the same system he ran ten years ago. It worked
  then. Opponents have since figured it out. He's the coach who's "figured out
  by week 8" every season because his tendencies never change.
- **Low adaptability**: This coach can't adjust — not within a game, and not
  across seasons. When the league shifts, he gets left behind. He's the retread
  who comes back and fails because the game he knew doesn't exist anymore.

This is invisible to you. What you see: "Our offense was top 5 for three
straight years and now we've fallen to 18th with the same roster." Is the coach
declining? Did opponents figure him out? Did the roster age? All three are
possible. You have to diagnose it yourself.

## The GM-Coach Relationship

This is where the coaching system intersects with the core GM fantasy. You are
the general manager. The coach works for you. But the relationship is more
complicated than employer-employee.

### Directives

You can issue season-long directives to your coaching staff. These are broad
strategic goals, not play-by-play instructions:

- **"Play the young guys"** — prioritize development; give rookies and 2nd-year
  players more reps even if veterans are slightly better right now
- **"Win now"** — maximize current-season performance; play the best players
  regardless of age or development considerations
- **"Establish the run"** — shift offensive philosophy toward a run-heavy
  approach
- **"Develop the rookie QB"** — start the rookie, live with the growing pains,
  build the offense around his development
- **"Be more aggressive"** — go for it on 4th down more often, blitz more, take
  deep shots

### How coaches respond to directives

Here's the thing: **coaches don't always listen.**

A coach's response to your directive depends on his hidden personality traits:

- **A coachable, low-ego coach** will generally follow your directives, even if
  he privately disagrees. He'll play the rookies. He'll run the ball more. He'll
  adjust.
- **A stubborn, high-ego coach** might ignore your directive entirely. You said
  "play the rookies" and he's still starting the 32-year-old veteran because
  _he_ thinks the veteran gives him the best chance to win. And honestly? He
  might be right. Or he might be protecting his win-loss record at the expense
  of your franchise's future.
- **A coach who partially complies** might give the rookie more snaps in
  blowouts but stick with the veteran in close games. He heard you. He just
  disagrees about how far to take it.

This tension is the game. A stubborn coach who wins is hard to fire. A compliant
coach who loses is easy to fire but might have been right to listen to you. A
coach who ignores your "develop the rookie" directive and starts the veteran —
and then the veteran gets hurt and the undeveloped rookie isn't ready — that's a
failure you both own.

### Trust and friction

Over time, the GM-coach relationship develops a dynamic:

- **Aligned vision**: When you and your coach agree on direction, everything
  flows. You're drafting the players he wants, he's developing them the way you
  expected, the results are good. This is the best-case scenario.
- **Productive tension**: You disagree on some things, but the friction produces
  better outcomes. You push him to play young players; he pushes back on the
  ones who aren't ready. The compromise is better than either extreme.
- **Toxic friction**: You want to rebuild; he wants to win now. You keep
  drafting developmental prospects; he keeps benching them. Neither of you is
  getting what you want, and the results show it. Something has to give —
  usually, someone gets fired.

### Firing consequences

Firing a coach isn't free:

- **Contract buyout** — you owe the remaining money on his contract; firing a
  coach in year 2 of a 5-year deal is expensive
- **Staff disruption** — a fired HC often means his coordinators and some
  position coaches leave too (loyalty, coaching tree connections); you might
  lose your excellent DB coach because he was "the HC's guy"
- **Locker room impact** — players who were loyal to the fired coach may be
  upset; team morale can dip; veteran leaders who respected the coach might
  become disengaged
- **Reputation effects** — a team that fires coaches frequently develops a
  reputation as unstable; quality coaching candidates may prefer a more stable
  organization
- **Development disruption** — a new coach means a new scheme, which means
  players lose their scheme fit bonus and may need 1-2 seasons to adjust; your
  3rd-year WR who was thriving in the old system might regress in the new one
- **Organizational knowledge loss** — a long-tenured coach understands your
  roster deeply; a new hire starts from scratch evaluating your players, and his
  initial depth chart decisions may be wrong

## The Coaching Market

Coaches are hired, fired, and poached in a market that mirrors the real NFL
coaching carousel.

### The offseason carousel

After each season, the coaching market activates:

- **Firings**: Teams fire underperforming coaches (including NPC teams, who make
  their own firing decisions based on their AI personality and expectations)
- **Openings**: Head coaching, coordinator, and position coaching jobs become
  available across the league
- **Candidate pool**: Available candidates include fired coaches, current
  assistants on other teams, and (rarely) college coaches
- **Interview process**: You request interviews with candidates; they may accept
  or decline based on your organization's reputation, roster talent, job
  stability, and their own ambitions
- **Competition**: Multiple teams pursue the same candidates; a hot OC candidate
  might have 5 interview requests and choose the most attractive landing spot

### Why a coach might not choose you

Coaching candidates evaluate opportunities. A candidate might prefer another
team because:

- **Better roster** — he wants to win, and the other team has a franchise QB
- **More stability** — your team fires coaches every 2 years; the other team is
  patient
- **Scheme fit** — the other team's roster fits his preferred system
- **Connections** — he has relationships with the other team's staff or front
  office
- **Market size / profile** — some coaches want a big stage; others prefer less
  pressure
- **Contract terms** — the other team is offering more money or more years

### Poaching

Your coaching staff's success makes them targets:

- **Coordinator-to-HC pipeline**: A great coordinator on your staff will attract
  head coaching interest from other teams. This is the NFL's most reliable
  coaching pipeline, and it means your success can cost you your best coaches.
- **Position coach promotions**: A position coach who develops multiple Pro
  Bowlers will get coordinator offers from other teams.
- **Blocking rules**: You can promote an assistant to a higher role to retain
  him (e.g., give your rising OC a raise and more authority). But you can't
  block a lateral move forever — and you can never block a promotion (an
  assistant coordinator can't be blocked from taking a coordinator job; a
  coordinator can't be blocked from a head coaching job).
- **Coaching pipeline as asset**: Smart GMs recognize that developing coaches is
  both a strength and a cost. You get excellent coaching while they're on your
  staff, and you lose them when they succeed. The teams that handle this best
  are always developing the next wave of assistants — so when the OC leaves, the
  QB coach is ready to step up.

### Coaching contracts

Coaches sign contracts with terms:

- **Length**: Typically 3-6 years for head coaches, 2-4 for coordinators, 1-3
  for position coaches
- **Salary**: Varies by reputation, experience, and market demand; impacts your
  coaching budget
- **Buyout**: The amount owed if you fire the coach before the contract expires
- **No guaranteed role changes**: A contract doesn't prevent a coach from being
  reassigned within your staff, but demotions may trigger morale and reputation
  consequences

**Contract incentives:**

Coaching contracts can include performance-based incentives that align the
coach's financial motivation with your organizational goals:

- **Playoff appearance bonus** — rewards making the postseason
- **Division title bonus** — rewards winning the division
- **Championship bonus** — the big payout for winning it all
- **Player development bonuses** — triggered when players under the coach's
  guidance hit milestones (Pro Bowl selections, stat thresholds, award
  nominations); these incentivize coaches to invest in development even when it
  doesn't immediately translate to wins
- **Win threshold bonuses** — triggered at specific win totals (e.g., bonus at
  10 wins, larger bonus at 12)

Incentives matter because they shape coaching behavior. A coach with a big
playoff bonus might prioritize winning now over developing rookies — even if you
issued a "play the young guys" directive. A coach with development bonuses might
actually listen to that directive because it's in his financial interest.
Structuring contracts with the right incentives is a subtle but powerful GM
tool.

Incentives also affect the hiring market. A coach choosing between two teams
might take less base salary from a team with a loaded roster if the incentive
package is rich — because he thinks he can earn it back by winning. A rebuilding
team might need to offer more guaranteed money because the incentives are
unlikely to pay out in year one.

### Staff budget

Every team operates under a fixed **staff budget** — a league-wide cap on
combined coaching and scouting salaries. The staff budget is completely separate
from the player salary cap: spending less on staff does not free up player cap
room, and vice versa. Every team gets the same budget, ensuring a level playing
field for football operations spending.

- **What counts** — annual base salaries of all coaches and scouts on the team's
  payroll, plus buyouts for fired staff in the year they're triggered
- **What doesn't count** — the player salary cap is entirely separate; the staff
  budget is an operating-expense ceiling, not a roster-construction tool
- **The diminishing returns question** — is the difference between a $3M OL
  coach and a $1.5M OL coach worth the extra spend? Maybe. You won't know until
  you've seen both work. And by then, the cheaper one might have been poached.
- **Staff depth vs. star power** — do you pay top dollar for an elite DC and
  fill the rest of the staff with cheap unknowns? Or do you spread the budget
  evenly for a solid-across-the-board staff?
- **The buyout tax** — firing a coach mid-contract means absorbing the buyout
  against your staff budget that year. A team that fires an expensive HC with
  three years left on a rich contract might not have room to hire a premium
  replacement until the buyout clears. This makes the firing decision weightier
  — you're not just losing a coach, you're burning budget.
- **Scouting vs. coaching tradeoff** — the same budget covers both scouts and
  coaches. A team that invests heavily in elite coordinators has less room for a
  deep scouting department, and vice versa. Elite scouting finds better draft
  talent; elite coaching develops it. The budget forces you to choose your
  organizational identity.

### College coaches

The coaching candidate pool includes college coaches — a high-risk, high-reward
segment of the market.

**Why hire a college coach:**

- **Scheme innovation** — college coaches are often at the cutting edge of
  offensive design; the spread revolution, RPOs, and tempo-based offenses all
  migrated from college to the NFL. A college coach might bring ideas the pros
  haven't seen yet.
- **Player development pedigree** — successful college coaches are, by
  definition, developers of young talent. They recruit 18-year-olds and turn
  them into NFL draft picks. That development skill can translate.
- **Fresh perspective** — a coach who's never been in the NFL has no
  NFL-specific bad habits, no "we've always done it this way" baggage, and no
  scar tissue from past NFL failures.
- **Affordability** — a college coordinator with no NFL track record is cheaper
  than a proven NFL coordinator; you're paying for potential, not pedigree.

**Why college coaches fail:**

- **The talent gap disappears.** In college, a top program has a roster full of
  future NFL players facing opponents who are mostly future accountants. Scheme
  can overcome talent deficits. In the NFL, every team has NFL talent. A scheme
  that "worked" in college because of a massive talent advantage might be
  ordinary when the talent is level.
- **NFL roster management is different.** College coaches recruit; NFL coaches
  don't. The practice structure, game preparation timeline, and player
  relationship dynamics are fundamentally different. Some college coaches adapt.
  Others never adjust to having 53 grown men with agents instead of 85
  scholarship kids.
- **Ego and culture shock.** A coach who was king of his college program — total
  control, adoring fans, nobody above him — may struggle with the pro power
  structure. He answers to you, the owner/GM. His players have more leverage.
  The media is harsher. Not every college king survives the transition.
- **Scheme translation isn't automatic.** The college game and the pro game are
  different sports in important ways. Wider hash marks, different rules, faster
  defenders, more complex coverages. An offense that gashed college defenses
  might be schematically unsound against NFL talent.

**The college coach gamble:**

Some of the greatest NFL coaches came from college (Jimmy Johnson, Barry
Switzer, Pete Carroll's second act). Many more flamed out spectacularly (Nick
Saban with the Dolphins, Steve Spurrier with Washington, Bobby Petrino with the
Falcons, Chip Kelly's decline in Philadelphia).

The hidden attributes tell the story. A college coach with high adaptability,
high innovation, and the mental makeup to handle the NFL's power dynamics can
thrive. One with low adaptability and high ego — no matter how many college
championships he won — is likely to struggle.

You'll get signals in the interview. But you won't know the truth until he's on
your sideline, and by then you've committed years and dollars. That's the bet.

## Interaction with Other Systems

### Player Development

Coaches are the primary engine of player development. The interaction:

- A position coach's hidden development attributes directly affect the
  development rate of every player in his group
- The coordinator's scheme stability affects how quickly players master the
  system
- The HC's culture and discipline attributes affect team-wide development
  factors (work ethic, consistency)
- A player's development is the product of his own hidden potential, his work
  ethic, and the quality of coaching he receives — all three must align for
  maximum growth

### Scheme and Strategy

Coaches own the scheme. This means:

- Your offensive scheme is whatever your OC (or play-calling HC) installs
- Your defensive scheme is whatever your DC (or play-calling HC) installs
- Scheme quality depends on the coach's scheme mastery, the roster's fit, and
  how long the system has been in place
- Scheme changes happen when you change coaches — and scheme changes are costly
  (see [Schemes & Strategy](./schemes-and-strategy.md))

### Depth Chart

Coaches set the depth chart — not you. This is the same principle described in
[Player Attributes](./player-attributes.md): the depth chart is coach output,
not user input. A coach's player evaluation accuracy determines whether the
right players are starting. His biases determine whether he's overvaluing
certain traits. His stubbornness determines whether he'll bench a declining
veteran in favor of a rising rookie.

### Scouting and Roster Building

Coaches provide input on roster needs, but their input is filtered through their
biases and evaluation accuracy:

- A coach might tell you "I need a speed receiver" when what his offense
  actually needs is a possession receiver — because he overvalues speed
- A coach might push to draft a player from his former team or coaching tree
  (loyalty bias)
- A coach's roster input is one signal among many — your scouts provide another,
  and the stats provide a third

### Game Simulation

On game day, the coaching staff's hidden attributes drive:

- Play selection and tendencies
- Halftime adjustments
- Clock management decisions
- How well the scheme is executed (a great scheme poorly coached is worse than
  an average scheme well coached)
- In-game decisions that can swing outcomes (4th down calls, timeout usage,
  challenge decisions)

## League Setup

This document describes coaching in a mature Zone Blitz league — candidates
cycling through the annual coaching carousel, reputations accrued across
seasons, coaching trees traced through a league with real history.

A brand-new league starts without any of that. See
[League Setup](./league-setup.md); the coaching-specific points:

- **Every coach is generated uniquely per league.** When a league is created,
  its entire coaching universe is generated alongside it. No recurring names
  across save files, no shared pool between leagues. The head coach you hire
  today exists in this league and only this league — a one-of-one with a career
  arc that will only ever play out here.
- **Setup-phase hiring is contested.** A single shared candidate pool is
  released at league setup, and every franchise (human-run and NPC alike) hires
  from it at the same time. If you wait on an elite coordinator, an NPC
  franchise may hire them first. Candidates have preferences — market tier,
  philosophy, existing staff — and make real choices about where to sign.
  Multiplayer can produce real-time bidding wars between two human operators for
  the same head coach.
- **Gems and busts apply to coaches too.** The uncertainty that shapes the
  allocation draft applies to staff. The acclaimed veteran may prove rigid. The
  unheralded position coach may become a future hall-of-fame head coach if
  someone takes the chance. Your front office is a draft pick, and it hits or
  misses the same way a first-round player selection does.
- **No prior coaching tree yet.** The first league-generated coaches become the
  owners of the league's coaching trees. Over decades, the next generations
  descend from them — but in Year 1, there are no "Shanahan disciples" or
  "Belichick branches" yet. That heritage has to be built.

## What Makes Coaching Fun

The coaching system is fun because it creates the most consequential and most
agonizing decisions in the game:

- **"Do I fire a coach my players love because we're losing?"** — the
  development is great, the game-day results aren't. There's no right answer.
- **"My OC just got a head coaching offer. Do I let him go or promote him?"** —
  but promoting him means changing your HC, and the HC might be fine.
- **"The hot coaching candidate chose the other team over me."** — because your
  organization fires coaches too often. Now you're stuck with your second
  choice. That reputation cost is real.
- **"I hired a retread and he's actually working."** — the league wrote him off,
  you saw something, and he's adapted. Your contrarian bet is paying off. For
  now.
- **"My coach is ignoring my rebuild directive and winning games."** — do you
  fire a winning coach because he won't play the rookies? What if winning is
  actually the right call and your rebuild plan was wrong?
- **"I've had the same coaching staff for 8 years and we've made the playoffs 6
  times. But this year we went 7-10 and the offense looks stale."** — is this a
  blip, or has the league figured us out? Do you blow up something that's worked
  for nearly a decade?

The deepest satisfaction comes from building a coaching staff that develops
players, wins games, and sustains excellence over years — and from the hard
calls you make when it stops working.
