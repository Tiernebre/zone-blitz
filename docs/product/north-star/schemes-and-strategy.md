# Schemes & Strategy

Football schemes are the connective tissue of the entire game. Your team's
scheme defines what kind of players you need, which prospects you value, which
free agents you target, and how your team performs on the field. But you don't
pick a scheme from a menu — **your coaches determine your scheme**. You want to
run a 3-4 defense? Hire a DC who runs a 3-4. You want a spread offense? Hire an
OC who installs one. Your scheme decision is a coaching hire decision, and
changing your scheme means changing your coaches — which ripples through every
other system.

## Why Schemes Matter

In a game without schemes, team-building is simple: get the highest-rated
players at every position. Schemes make it a puzzle. A player with elite
attributes who doesn't fit your scheme might perform worse than a player with
good-not-great attributes who fits perfectly. This means:

- Two GMs looking at the same draft class build completely different boards
- A player's trade value depends on who's buying
- Coaching hires have multi-year roster implications
- There's no single "correct" way to build a team

## Schemes Are Tendencies, Not Labels

The modern NFL doesn't run pure schemes. Nobody is a "pure 3-4 team" or a "pure
West Coast offense" anymore. The distinction between a 4-3 defensive end and a
3-4 outside linebacker has blurred into "edge rusher." Most teams play nickel as
their base defense. Offenses blend concepts from multiple traditions on every
drive.

**In this game, a coach doesn't select a scheme from a dropdown.** A coach
brings a **set of tendencies, principles, and preferences** that define how his
unit plays football. The "scheme" is the aggregate of these tendencies — a
fingerprint, not a label.

### Offensive tendencies

An offensive coordinator's identity is defined by where he falls on several
spectrums:

**Run/pass philosophy:**

- Run-heavy ←→ Pass-heavy — the fundamental orientation; a run-heavy OC might
  target 55-60% run plays; a pass-heavy OC might throw on 65%+ of plays
- Early-down run tendency — some OCs establish the run on 1st and 2nd down and
  throw on 3rd; others spread you out from the first snap

**Personnel philosophy:**

- Light ←→ Heavy — does the OC prefer 11 personnel (1 RB, 1 TE, 3 WR) or
  12/21/22 personnel (multiple TEs and/or RBs)? This determines which position
  groups matter most for your roster.
- TE usage — is the tight end a glorified offensive lineman, a primary receiving
  weapon, or a versatile H-back who does everything? This single tendency can
  make or break a TE's career on your team.
- RB usage — workhorse back who gets 25 carries, committee approach with
  specialized roles, or receiving back who's basically a slot receiver?

**Tempo:**

- Slow and methodical ←→ Up-tempo / no-huddle — affects play volume, fatigue,
  time of possession, and the kind of QB you need (a no-huddle QB must process
  fast and manage the line of scrimmage himself)

**Formation philosophy:**

- Under center ←→ Shotgun/pistol — affects run game style, play-action
  effectiveness, and QB skill set requirements
- Formation diversity — does the OC line up in 15 different formations to create
  confusion, or does he run the same 3-4 looks and execute perfectly? Diverse
  formations stress the defense but require smarter, more versatile players.
- Pre-snap motion — heavy motion (Shanahan-tree, McVay-tree) creates diagnostic
  information and mismatches but adds complexity

**Passing philosophy:**

- Timing-based ←→ Improvisation-based — a timing offense needs precision and
  rhythm (West Coast, Air Raid); an improvisation-based offense needs a QB who
  can extend plays and receivers who can win off-script
- Short/intermediate ←→ Vertical — does the OC attack underneath and let
  receivers run after the catch, or push the ball downfield and take shots?
- Progression reads ←→ Simplified reads — a full-field progression offense
  (pro-style) requires elite processing; a half-field or single-read offense
  (RPO, Air Raid) simplifies the QB's job but limits options

**Run game philosophy:**

- Zone ←→ Gap/power — the fundamental blocking scheme distinction; zone schemes
  want athletic, mobile linemen who can reach and climb; gap/power schemes want
  big, strong linemen who can drive defenders off the ball
- Inside ←→ Outside — does the run game attack between the tackles or on the
  perimeter? Inside zone, outside zone, power, counter, stretch — each demands
  different OL and RB attributes
- RPO integration — how heavily does the OC attach run-pass options to the run
  game? Heavy RPO usage requires a QB with elite processing and an OL that can
  handle the ambiguity of blocking for both run and pass

### Defensive tendencies

A defensive coordinator's identity is similarly defined by spectrums:

**Front philosophy:**

- Odd ←→ Even front — three down linemen (odd) vs. four (even); but in modern
  football, the distinction is blurry because nickel packages change the math on
  every passing down
- One-gap ←→ Two-gap — do your linemen attack a single gap (penetration,
  disruption) or control two gaps (space-eating, discipline)? This is the most
  important DL personnel distinction in the game.
- Edge rush philosophy — speed rush off the edge, power rush, or versatile
  rushers who can do both? A DC who wants speed rushers needs a very different
  edge player than one who wants power-setting edge defenders.

**Personnel philosophy:**

- Base ←→ Sub-package heavy — does the DC play base defense (3 LBs or 4 DL) on
  early downs, or does he live in nickel/dime even against the run? The modern
  NFL trend is toward lighter, faster defenses — but a DC who commits to nickel
  as his base needs corners and safeties who can also tackle.
- Safety usage — are safeties deep-field ball hawks, or does the DC bring
  safeties into the box as extra run defenders and blitzers? The "big nickel" (3
  safeties) and "Star" packages are modern wrinkles.
- Linebacker versatility — does the DC need LBs who can cover (modern,
  pass-defense-oriented) or LBs who fill gaps and tackle (traditional,
  run-defense-oriented)?

**Coverage philosophy:**

- Man ←→ Zone — the fundamental coverage spectrum; man coverage requires elite
  individual cover talent; zone coverage requires discipline, communication, and
  football IQ
- Single-high ←→ Two-high — one deep safety (Cover 1, Cover 3) or two deep
  safeties (Cover 2, Cover 4/Quarters)? This changes everything about how the
  defense defends the run and the deep ball.
- Press ←→ Off — do corners press at the line (disrupts timing but risks getting
  beaten) or play off (safer but gives up easy completions)? A press-heavy DC
  needs corners with elite physical tools and technique.

**Pressure philosophy:**

- Four-man rush ←→ Blitz-heavy — does the DC trust his front four to generate
  pressure, or does he send 5-6 on a regular basis? A blitz-heavy DC needs cover
  corners who can survive 1-on-1, because every blitzer is one fewer defender in
  coverage.
- Disguise — does the DC show his pressure looks pre-snap, or does he disguise
  and rotate at the snap? Disguise creates confusion but requires smarter, more
  disciplined players.
- Zone blitz usage — dropping linemen into coverage while blitzing from
  unexpected positions; a niche but effective tendency that requires versatile,
  high-IQ defenders

**Aggressiveness:**

- Conservative ←→ Aggressive — overall risk tolerance; an aggressive DC gambles
  more (press, blitz, tight coverage) for higher upside but bigger breakdowns; a
  conservative DC bends but doesn't break, limiting big plays at the cost of
  allowing methodical drives
- Situational aggression — some DCs are conservative on early downs but
  aggressive on 3rd down; others dial up pressure when they have a lead but play
  soft from behind

### What this means for roster building

Two coordinators who both get called "zone defense guys" by the media might want
completely different rosters:

```
DC A — "Zone defense coordinator"
  Coverage: Two-high zone (Cover 2 / Cover 4)
  Front: Even, one-gap penetration
  Pressure: Four-man rush, rarely blitzes
  Personnel: Nickel base, athletic LBs who cover

  Needs: Rangy safeties, physical corners who play flats, a dominant
  interior pass rusher (3-technique), coverage LBs

DC B — "Zone defense coordinator"
  Coverage: Single-high zone (Cover 3), heavy disguise
  Front: Odd, two-gap with edge speed
  Pressure: Moderate blitz, zone blitz concepts
  Personnel: Base 3-4 on early downs, nickel on passing downs

  Needs: Space-eating nose tackle, versatile OLBs, an elite free safety,
  smart defenders who can handle assignment complexity
```

Same label. Completely different rosters. This is why the coaching hire IS the
scheme decision — and why "what scheme do we run?" is actually dozens of smaller
questions about tendencies and principles.

## Foundational Concepts

While coaches blend and customize, their tendencies draw from established
football concepts. These are the building blocks — the vocabulary of the game. A
coach's system is a combination of these elements, weighted by his preferences
and adapted to his personnel.

### Offensive foundations

**West Coast offense:** The Bill Walsh revolution. Short, precise passing
replaces the running game as the ball-control mechanism. Timing routes, yards
after catch, methodical drives. The foundation for much of modern passing.
Demands accuracy, route precision, and receivers who make people miss after the
catch.

**Air Raid / spread passing:** Simplified route concepts, high tempo, spread
formations. The Mike Leach tree and its descendants. Four or five wide
receivers, spread the defense horizontally, attack with speed and space. The
college-to-pro pipeline has brought Air Raid concepts into almost every NFL
offense. Demands quick decision-making from the QB and receivers who win in
space.

**Pro-style / multiple:** The traditional NFL offense. Balanced run/pass,
multiple formations, relies on execution and versatility. Under center,
play-action, deep drops, full- field reads. Requires a well-rounded roster — no
single weak link can hide. The most demanding system for a quarterback because
nothing is simplified.

**Zone run scheme:** Outside zone, inside zone, wide zone — a family of run
concepts built on athletic offensive linemen who reach, climb, and create
movement at the point of attack. The runner reads the blocks and cuts
accordingly. The Shanahan/Kubiak tree popularized this approach. Demands mobile,
technically sound linemen and patient, decisive runners with vision.

**Gap/power run scheme:** Power, counter, trap, pull — a family of run concepts
built on creating numerical advantages at the point of attack through pulling
linemen and lead blockers. Demands strong, physical linemen and downhill runners
who can press the hole and finish runs.

**RPO integration:** Run-pass options attached to the run game. The QB reads a
defensive player post-snap and decides to hand off, throw a quick pass, or (in
some designs) pull and throw deeper. A modern staple that most offenses now
incorporate to some degree. Demands a QB with elite processing speed and an OL
that can handle the ambiguity.

**Option concepts:** Read-option, triple-option, zone-read, and related concepts
that use the QB as a running threat. A continuum from pure option (flexbone,
service academies) to modern zone-read concepts that most mobile QBs now use.
Demands an athletic QB willing to take hits.

**Motion and misdirection:** Pre-snap motion, jet sweeps, play-action — concepts
that manipulate defenders' eyes and create false keys. Heavy-motion offenses
(Shanahan, McVay, McDaniel) use motion diagnostically (reading how the defense
adjusts) and schematically (creating blocks, pulling defenders out of position).
Adds complexity but creates mismatches.

### Defensive foundations

**Even front (4-down):** Four down linemen, typically two defensive tackles and
two defensive ends. The classic NFL front. Generates pass rush from the front
four, strong against the run, simple gap assignments. Modern variants include
the wide-9 alignment (edges set wide for speed rushing).

**Odd front (3-down):** Three down linemen with four linebackers. More versatile
pre-snap because the defense can show multiple looks — any of the four LBs might
rush, drop, or blitz. Demands a space-eating nose tackle and hybrid edge/OLB
types.

**Nickel / sub-package:** Extra defensive backs replace linebackers. In the
modern NFL, most teams play nickel (5 DBs) on 60%+ of snaps, making it
effectively the base defense. Some teams go even further with dime (6 DBs) or
"big nickel" (3 safeties). Demands quality DB depth and versatile safeties.

**Cover 2 / Tampa 2:** Two deep safeties split the field. Corners play the
flats. In Tampa 2, the MLB drops deep into the middle zone, requiring an
athletic LB who can run. Limits big plays but can be vulnerable between the
safeties and to seam routes.

**Cover 3:** Single high safety, three deep zones (safety and two corners).
Strong against vertical passing, puts an extra defender in the box for run
support. Vulnerable underneath, especially to short/intermediate routes in the
flat and curl zones.

**Cover 4 / Quarters:** Four defenders split the deep field into quarters. A
two-high shell that is strong against both the run and the pass. The modern
"umbrella" defense that many teams now use as their base coverage. Adaptable but
requires smart, disciplined defenders.

**Man coverage / press-man:** Individual matchups. Each defender is responsible
for a specific receiver. Press technique at the line disrupts timing but risks
getting beaten by speed or release moves. Demands elite individual talent in the
secondary — you can't scheme around a corner who loses his matchup.

**Zone blitz:** The Dick LeBeau innovation. Drop defensive linemen into
coverage, blitz linebackers and defensive backs from unexpected positions.
Creates confusion in blocking assignments and quarterback reads. Demands
versatile, high-IQ defenders who can handle unusual responsibilities.

## Player Archetypes

Archetypes are the bridge between raw attributes and scheme fit. An archetype is
a **role within a scheme** defined by the attribute profile it demands. The same
position on the roster might house very different archetypes depending on what
the coach's tendencies require.

A player doesn't have a fixed archetype label. A player has attributes. The
archetype describes what the scheme needs, and the fit — or lack of fit —
emerges from whether the player's attributes match the role's demands.

### Quarterback archetypes

**Pocket passer:** Lives in the pocket, wins with arm talent, accuracy,
anticipation, and pre-snap reads. The classic NFL QB model.

- **Primary attributes**: Accuracy (short, medium, deep), arm strength, football
  IQ, decision-making, anticipation, clutch
- **Secondary attributes**: Touch, release, pocket awareness
- **Physical profile**: Size matters (taller QBs see the field better), speed is
  irrelevant
- **Scheme fit**: Pro-style, West Coast, timing-based passing offenses; these
  QBs thrive in systems with full-field reads and structured progressions
- **Example archetype**: The 6'4" QB who can't run but carves up defenses from
  the pocket with pinpoint accuracy and preternatural anticipation

**Dual-threat QB:** Dangerous with both his arm and his legs. Can extend plays,
pick up first downs on scrambles, and force defenses to account for the run.

- **Primary attributes**: Speed, acceleration, agility, accuracy (on the run),
  decision-making, football IQ
- **Secondary attributes**: Arm strength, elusiveness, composure
- **Physical profile**: Athletic, mobile, may sacrifice size for speed
- **Scheme fit**: RPO-heavy, option concepts, zone-read, pistol; systems that
  use the QB as a running threat and incorporate designed QB runs
- **Risk factor**: Dual-threat QBs take more hits; durability is a hidden
  concern that can end careers early

**Game manager:** Doesn't make the spectacular throw, but doesn't lose games
either. Gets the ball to playmakers, protects possession, and executes the
scheme reliably.

- **Primary attributes**: Decision-making, composure, consistency, accuracy
  (short), football IQ
- **Secondary attributes**: Touch, leadership
- **Physical profile**: Doesn't matter — this archetype is defined by mental
  attributes, not physical ones
- **Scheme fit**: Power run offenses, strong-defense teams, any system where the
  QB isn't asked to carry the offense; the game manager's value is inversely
  proportional to how much the offense depends on QB play
- **The undervalued archetype**: In a league that chases the next franchise QB,
  a game manager on a cheap contract with an elite defense and running game
  might be the smartest roster construction in the league

**Gunslinger:** High-risk, high-reward arm talent. Launches bombs, throws into
tight windows, makes jaw-dropping plays — and backbreaking turnovers.

- **Primary attributes**: Arm strength, accuracy (deep), clutch (or lack thereof
  — a gunslinger with low clutch is a disaster in big moments)
- **Secondary attributes**: Touch (often lacking), decision-making (the key
  variable — a gunslinger with elite decision-making is a franchise QB; one with
  poor decision-making is a turnover machine)
- **Scheme fit**: Vertical passing offenses, Air Raid, any system that pushes
  the ball downfield; badly fits conservative, ball-control systems
- **The coaching challenge**: Can you coach the gunslinger to throw it away? Can
  you give him a system that channels his aggression productively? This
  archetype is the ultimate coaching test.

### Running back archetypes

**Power / downhill back:** Between-the-tackles runner who punishes defenders.
Falls forward, breaks tackles, wears defenses down in the 4th quarter.

- **Primary attributes**: Strength, ball carrying, durability, stamina
- **Secondary attributes**: Speed (enough to hit the hole, not necessarily
  breakaway), acceleration
- **Physical profile**: 220-240 lbs, thick build, low center of gravity
- **Scheme fit**: Gap/power run schemes, short-yardage/goal-line packages,
  cold-weather and late-season football

**Scat back / receiving back:** A weapon in the passing game. Lines up in the
slot, runs routes, catches the ball in space, creates mismatches against
linebackers.

- **Primary attributes**: Catching, route running, elusiveness, run after catch,
  agility
- **Secondary attributes**: Speed, acceleration
- **Physical profile**: 185-205 lbs, quick, fluid
- **Scheme fit**: West Coast, spread, pass-heavy offenses, any system that uses
  the RB as a receiver; often a change-of-pace/third-down specialist

**Zone runner:** Patient, one-cut runner with elite vision. Reads blocks, sets
up defenders, and hits the crease hard.

- **Primary attributes**: Football IQ (vision), acceleration, agility, ball
  carrying
- **Secondary attributes**: Speed, elusiveness
- **Physical profile**: Varies — the key is processing speed and lateral
  quickness, not a specific body type
- **Scheme fit**: Zone run schemes (inside zone, outside zone, wide zone); a
  zone runner in a gap scheme may underperform because his patience reads as
  indecisiveness

**All-purpose back:** Does everything well, nothing at an elite level. Runs,
catches, blocks, stays on the field for all three downs.

- **Primary attributes**: Balanced profile — ball carrying, catching, pass
  blocking all need to be solid
- **Secondary attributes**: Durability (three-down backs take a beating),
  stamina
- **Scheme fit**: Pro-style, any system that doesn't substitute heavily; the
  all-purpose back's value is that you don't have to tip your play call with
  personnel changes

### Wide receiver archetypes

**X receiver (flanker / boundary receiver):** The alpha. Lines up on the
outside, wins contested catches, high-points the football, and dominates
one-on-one matchups. He's the "go get it" receiver you throw to in single
coverage.

- **Primary attributes**: Contested catching, catching, route running, speed
- **Secondary attributes**: Jumping, strength, size
- **Physical profile**: 6'1"+ and 200+ lbs; size and length matter here because
  this receiver plays above the rim
- **Scheme fit**: Any offense needs an X, but vertical passing systems and
  offenses that isolate receivers benefit most; a coach who runs heavy
  play-action with deep shots needs an elite X

**Z receiver (split end / field receiver):** The versatile outside receiver. May
line up on either side, often in motion. Wins with speed, route running, and the
ability to threaten at every level of the field.

- **Primary attributes**: Route running, speed, acceleration, catching
- **Secondary attributes**: Run after catch, elusiveness
- **Physical profile**: Varies — the Z is defined by skill, not body type
- **Scheme fit**: Motion-heavy offenses (Shanahan tree), systems that use
  pre-snap movement diagnostically, offenses that need speed to stretch the
  field horizontally

**Slot receiver:** Works the middle of the field. Quick releases off the line,
sharp route running, catches in traffic, creates yards after the catch in space.

- **Primary attributes**: Route running, catching, run after catch, agility,
  acceleration
- **Secondary attributes**: Clutch (catching over the middle with a safety
  coming), toughness
- **Physical profile**: 5'9"-6'0", 180-200 lbs typically; quickness over size
- **Scheme fit**: West Coast, spread, any offense that attacks the short/
  intermediate middle of the field; some offenses live in 11 personnel and the
  slot is the most important receiver on the team

**Deep threat / speedster:** One thing: run fast. Stretches the defense
vertically, opens up underneath routes for everyone else, and occasionally
catches a bomb for a touchdown.

- **Primary attributes**: Speed, acceleration, catching (particularly deep ball
  tracking)
- **Secondary attributes**: Route running (limited tree — mostly go routes,
  posts, and deep crossers)
- **Physical profile**: Fast. That's it. Can be small if he's fast enough.
- **Scheme fit**: Vertical passing offenses, offenses that need to create space
  underneath; even a conservative offense benefits from a deep threat who forces
  the safety to respect the deep ball
- **The one-trick question**: A pure deep threat who can't run other routes
  becomes predictable. Defenses bracket him and take away the deep ball. His
  value depends on whether the rest of the offense can punish the defense for
  devoting resources to stopping him.

### Tight end archetypes

**Blocking tight end / Y tight end:** An extra offensive lineman who happens to
line up at tight end. Sets the edge in the run game, chips pass rushers, and
occasionally leaks out for a play-action target.

- **Primary attributes**: Run blocking, pass blocking, strength
- **Secondary attributes**: Catching (enough to be a credible decoy)
- **Physical profile**: 6'4"+, 255+ lbs, built like a lineman
- **Scheme fit**: Power run, pro-style, any offense that values a physical run
  game at the point of attack; essential in 12 and 13 personnel groupings

**Receiving tight end / move tight end:** A matchup nightmare. Lines up inline,
in the slot, split out wide — wherever he can exploit a coverage mismatch. Too
big for corners, too fast for linebackers.

- **Primary attributes**: Route running, catching, run after catch, speed,
  agility
- **Secondary attributes**: Contested catching, football IQ
- **Physical profile**: 6'3"-6'6", 230-250 lbs; athletic enough to win as a
  receiver, big enough that you can't cover him with a DB
- **Scheme fit**: Modern passing offenses, any system that creates mismatches in
  the seam and over the middle; the receiving TE is the most scheme-transcendent
  offensive weapon — he warps coverage by existing

**H-back / fullback-TE hybrid:** Lines up everywhere — inline, in the backfield,
in the slot, in motion. Does a little of everything: lead blocks, catches
checkdowns, chips defenders, runs option routes. A Swiss Army knife.

- **Primary attributes**: Run blocking, catching, football IQ (must know
  multiple alignment responsibilities), agility
- **Secondary attributes**: Pass blocking, ball carrying
- **Physical profile**: 6'1"-6'4", 240-255 lbs; not the biggest, not the
  fastest, but smart and versatile
- **Scheme fit**: Shanahan-tree offenses, motion-heavy systems, offenses that
  use pre-snap alignment versatility to create diagnostic reads; the H-back's
  value is that the defense doesn't know what he's doing on any given play

**Fullback / lead blocker:** The dinosaur that refuses to go extinct. A pure
lead blocker who lines up in the backfield and clears a path for the running
back. In a league that has nearly abandoned the position, carrying a fullback is
a philosophical statement about how you want to play football.

- **Primary attributes**: Run blocking, strength, durability
- **Secondary attributes**: Ball carrying (short-yardage dive plays), catching
  (enough to be a play-action threat out of the flat)
- **Physical profile**: 5'11"-6'2", 240-260 lbs; built like a bowling ball
- **Scheme fit**: Power run, gap schemes, goal-line packages, any offense that
  uses 21 or 22 personnel groupings; the fullback is nearly useless in spread or
  pass-heavy schemes, which is why most teams don't carry one
- **The roster spot question**: This is the real debate. Carrying a fullback
  means _not_ carrying a 6th WR, a 4th safety, or an extra pass rusher. If your
  fullback plays 10 offensive snaps per game, is that roster spot worth it? In a
  power run offense, absolutely — he's the lead blocker on your most important
  plays. In a spread offense, he's a wasted roster spot. The fullback is the
  purest example of how scheme dictates roster construction.

### Offensive line archetypes

**Mauler / road grader:** Physically dominant run blocker. Drives defenders off
the ball, creates movement at the point of attack, finishes blocks in the dirt.

- **Primary attributes**: Strength, run blocking, durability
- **Secondary attributes**: Stamina, size
- **Physical profile**: 6'4"+, 315+ lbs, massive
- **Scheme fit**: Gap/power run schemes, ground-and-pound offenses, any system
  that wins by physically imposing its will at the line of scrimmage
- **Trade-off**: Maulers often struggle in pass protection because their anchor
  relies on aggression and forward lean — which pass rushers can exploit with
  counter moves

**Technician / pass protector:** Wins with footwork, hand placement, and
leverage rather than raw power. Mirrors speed rushers, anchors against bull
rushes with technique, and rarely gets beaten because his fundamentals are
sound.

- **Primary attributes**: Pass blocking, agility, football IQ
- **Secondary attributes**: Strength (enough to anchor), run blocking
- **Physical profile**: Varies — the technician is defined by technique, not
  body type
- **Scheme fit**: Pass-heavy offenses, spread, Air Raid; any system that drops
  back 40+ times per game needs technicians who can sustain protection

**Athletic / zone blocker:** Moves well laterally, reaches to the second level,
climbs to linebackers, and combo blocks effectively. The modern NFL lineman.

- **Primary attributes**: Agility, acceleration, run blocking, football IQ
- **Secondary attributes**: Pass blocking, strength
- **Physical profile**: 6'3"-6'6", 300-320 lbs; lighter and more athletic than a
  mauler
- **Scheme fit**: Zone run schemes (inside zone, outside zone, wide zone); this
  archetype is increasingly dominant as zone concepts have taken over the NFL; a
  zone blocker in a gap/power scheme may underperform because his strength
  doesn't match the assignment demands

**Versatile / swing lineman:** Can play multiple positions along the line. The
ultimate roster flexibility piece — he might be your starting right guard and
your backup left tackle.

- **Primary attributes**: Football IQ (must know multiple positions'
  assignments), pass blocking, run blocking — all need to be solid, none need to
  be elite
- **Secondary attributes**: Agility, strength
- **Scheme fit**: Any system values versatility, but it's particularly important
  for teams with roster constraints or injury concerns; a versatile lineman
  might be your 6th OL who dresses on game day and can step in at any of the
  five positions

### Defensive line archetypes

**Edge rusher / speed rusher:** Gets to the quarterback with speed, bend, and
pass rush moves. The most valuable non-QB archetype in football because sacks
change games.

- **Primary attributes**: Speed, acceleration, pass rushing, agility
- **Secondary attributes**: Strength (enough to hold the edge), stamina
- **Physical profile**: 6'2"-6'5", 240-265 lbs; built for speed off the edge
- **Scheme fit**: Any defense that generates pressure from the edge; speed
  rushers are particularly valuable in four-man rush systems where the DC trusts
  his front to create pressure without blitzing

**Power edge / edge setter:** Wins with strength and power rather than speed.
Sets the edge against the run, bull-rushes offensive tackles, and collapses the
pocket. Less flashy than the speed rusher, but more complete.

- **Primary attributes**: Strength, run defense, pass rushing, block shedding
- **Secondary attributes**: Durability, stamina
- **Physical profile**: 6'3"-6'6", 265-285 lbs; bigger and more physical than
  the speed rusher
- **Scheme fit**: Two-gap defenses, run-first defensive fronts, teams that
  prioritize stopping the run; also valuable as the complementary edge opposite
  a speed rusher

**3-technique / penetrating DT:** The interior disruptor. Lined up between the
guard and tackle, he shoots gaps, collapses the pocket from the inside, and
blows up run plays in the backfield. Aaron Donald is the archetype.

- **Primary attributes**: Acceleration, pass rushing, agility, strength
- **Secondary attributes**: Block shedding, speed (for a DT)
- **Physical profile**: 6'1"-6'4", 280-305 lbs; undersized for a DT but
  explosively quick
- **Scheme fit**: One-gap, penetration-based defenses; the 3-technique is the
  engine of an aggressive defensive line; less valuable in two-gap systems that
  ask DTs to hold ground rather than penetrate

**Nose tackle / space eater:** Occupies two gaps, absorbs double teams, and
clogs the middle of the field. Won't show up on the stat sheet. Will show up on
the film as the reason your linebackers are running free to make tackles.

- **Primary attributes**: Strength, run defense, durability, size
- **Secondary attributes**: Block shedding, stamina
- **Physical profile**: 6'0"-6'4", 320-350 lbs; massive, immovable
- **Scheme fit**: Two-gap defenses, odd fronts (3-4), run-stuffing defensive
  philosophies; the nose tackle is the most scheme-dependent DL archetype — a
  great nose tackle in a penetrating defense is wasted because his strength
  (occupying space) isn't what the scheme asks for

### Linebacker archetypes

**Off-ball run thumper:** Fills gaps, takes on blocks, and makes tackles between
the tackles. The traditional middle linebacker who stops the run and doesn't
leave the box.

- **Primary attributes**: Tackling, run defense, block shedding, strength
- **Secondary attributes**: Football IQ, composure
- **Physical profile**: 6'1"-6'3", 240-255 lbs; big, physical, downhill
- **Scheme fit**: Run-first defenses, traditional 4-3 and 3-4 schemes; this
  archetype is increasingly rare in a passing league because he can't stay on
  the field in nickel — if he can't cover, he's a two-down player

**Coverage linebacker:** Runs with tight ends, drops into zones, plays the pass
as well as the run. The modern NFL linebacker who can stay on the field in
nickel packages because he doesn't create a coverage liability.

- **Primary attributes**: Zone coverage, man coverage, speed, agility, football
  IQ
- **Secondary attributes**: Tackling, run defense
- **Physical profile**: 6'0"-6'3", 225-245 lbs; lighter, faster, more fluid
- **Scheme fit**: Modern defenses, nickel-heavy systems, Cover 2/Tampa 2 (which
  demands an athletic MLB), any defense that faces passing offenses regularly
  (which in the modern NFL is every defense)
- **The replacement**: In many modern defenses, this archetype has replaced the
  run thumper entirely — the game has evolved to demand LBs who can cover, and
  run-stopping-only LBs have been schemed into obsolescence

**Edge / OLB hybrid:** Stands up on the edge in odd fronts, rushes the passer,
drops into coverage, and sets the edge against the run. The 3-4 OLB who does
everything.

- **Primary attributes**: Pass rushing, speed, run defense, man coverage (enough
  to handle a TE)
- **Secondary attributes**: Block shedding, agility
- **Physical profile**: 6'2"-6'5", 240-260 lbs; a tweener between DE and LB who
  fits neither traditional position cleanly
- **Scheme fit**: 3-4 defenses, odd fronts, multiple-look defenses that disguise
  their rush; this archetype's value is versatility — he can rush and drop, and
  the offense doesn't know which one pre-snap
- **The evaluation challenge**: This is the hardest archetype to scout because
  the player must be excellent at two very different skills. A pass-rushing
  specialist disguised as a hybrid is a common draft mistake.

### Secondary archetypes

**Boundary corner:** Lines up on the outside, typically on the defense's right
side (facing the offense's best receiver). Plays press or off coverage, tracks a
single receiver, and is responsible for his man or his deep third depending on
the call.

- **Primary attributes**: Man coverage, speed, agility, clutch
- **Secondary attributes**: Jumping, anticipation
- **Physical profile**: 5'11"-6'2", 185-200 lbs; length and speed matter; long
  arms and fluid hips are the physical hallmarks
- **Scheme fit**: Man-heavy defenses need elite boundary corners because there's
  nowhere to hide; zone defenses can mask a weaker boundary corner with safety
  help

**Slot corner / nickel back:** Covers the slot receiver. A different skill set
than the boundary corner — the slot defender works in traffic, covers
quick-breaking routes, tackles in space, and often faces the opponent's most
elusive receiver.

- **Primary attributes**: Zone coverage, agility, tackling, anticipation
- **Secondary attributes**: Man coverage, acceleration
- **Physical profile**: 5'9"-6'0", 185-200 lbs; quicker and more compact than a
  boundary corner; some teams use undersized safeties here
- **Scheme fit**: Any defense that plays nickel (which is every defense in the
  modern game); the slot corner is a starter, not a sub-package luxury; a weak
  slot corner is exploitable on 60%+ of defensive snaps
- **The hidden value**: Slot corner might be the most undervalued archetype in
  the game because fans and media focus on the outside corners. A GM who invests
  in elite slot coverage has a structural advantage.

**Free safety / centerfielder:** Plays deep, covers ground sideline to sideline,
reads the quarterback's eyes, and makes plays on the ball. The last line of
defense.

- **Primary attributes**: Zone coverage, speed, anticipation, football IQ,
  catching
- **Secondary attributes**: Jumping, clutch
- **Physical profile**: 5'11"-6'2", 195-210 lbs; range and ball skills matter
  more than size
- **Scheme fit**: Single-high defenses (Cover 1, Cover 3) that ask the free
  safety to patrol the deep middle; an elite free safety makes the entire
  defense better because he erases mistakes underneath

**Box safety / strong safety:** Plays closer to the line of scrimmage. Run
support, blitz weapon, covers tight ends, tackles in the open field. A hybrid
between safety and linebacker.

- **Primary attributes**: Tackling, run defense, man coverage, strength
- **Secondary attributes**: Zone coverage, speed, blitz ability
- **Physical profile**: 6'0"-6'3", 210-225 lbs; bigger and more physical than a
  free safety
- **Scheme fit**: Defenses that need a run defender in the secondary, 8-man box
  looks, teams that blitz from the safety position; the box safety's coverage
  limitations can be exposed in two-high schemes that ask him to play deep
- **The modern tension**: The NFL increasingly wants safeties who can play both
  deep and in the box. The pure box safety is being squeezed by the versatile
  "do-everything" safety archetype — but a box safety with elite run support and
  blitz ability still has value in the right scheme.

**Versatile safety / positionless DB:** The modern ideal. Can play deep, in the
box, in the slot, on the blitz — wherever the defense needs him on any given
play. The do-everything defensive back.

- **Primary attributes**: Zone coverage, man coverage, tackling, football IQ,
  speed
- **Secondary attributes**: Run defense, anticipation, composure
- **Physical profile**: 6'0"-6'2", 200-215 lbs; athletic enough to cover,
  physical enough to tackle, smart enough to play multiple positions
- **Scheme fit**: Any defense, but particularly valuable in disguise-heavy
  systems where pre-snap alignment is designed to confuse the offense; the
  versatile safety lets the DC show one look and play another

### Special teams archetypes

**Power kicker:** Big leg. Makes 55-yarders. Might miss the 38-yarder because
accuracy isn't his thing.

- **Primary attributes**: Kicking power
- **Secondary attributes**: Kicking accuracy, clutch
- **Scheme fit**: Teams that play in domes or warm weather, offenses that stall
  in the red zone (more long FG attempts), aggressive coaches who want the
  option to kick from 55+

**Precision kicker:** Won't miss inside 45. Won't attempt from 55. Reliable,
consistent, boring in the best possible way.

- **Primary attributes**: Kicking accuracy, clutch, consistency
- **Secondary attributes**: Kicking power
- **Scheme fit**: Any team, but particularly valuable for conservative coaches
  and playoff-caliber teams where a missed kick is the difference between a
  Super Bowl and a "what if"

**Punter — directional / weapon:** Pins opponents inside the 10. Varies hang
time, direction, and distance to control field position. Doesn't just kick it
far — kicks it smart.

- **Primary attributes**: Punting accuracy, punting power, football IQ
- **Secondary attributes**: Clutch (coffin corner punts under pressure)
- **Scheme fit**: Teams with elite defenses that win the field position battle;
  a great punter on a great defense is a cheat code because every opponent drive
  starts with a long field

**Long snapper:** You don't notice him when he's good. You absolutely notice him
when he's bad. A botched snap loses games.

- **Primary attributes**: Snap accuracy, consistency, clutch
- **Scheme fit**: Every team. The long snapper is the ultimate "you don't know
  what you have until it's gone" position.

**Return specialist:** Electric with the ball in his hands in the open field.
Turns a routine kickoff into a 50-yard return, turns a punt into a house call.
The return specialist might be a WR or DB who doubles in the role, or he might
be a roster spot dedicated purely to the return game.

- **Primary attributes**: Speed, acceleration, elusiveness, agility, ball
  carrying
- **Secondary attributes**: Clutch (fielding a punt with a gunner in your face),
  football IQ (knowing when to fair catch, when to let it go, when to take it
  out of the end zone)
- **Physical profile**: Fast and elusive; size is irrelevant — what matters is
  burst, vision, and the ability to make the first man miss
- **Scheme fit**: Every team benefits from a dangerous returner, but the
  question is whether you dedicate a roster spot to it or use a player who
  contributes elsewhere. A return specialist who can't play offense or defense
  is a luxury roster spot — worth it if he's elite, a waste if he's merely good.
  Some STCs design their return schemes around the returner's skill set (speed
  returner gets schemes that set up the sideline; elusive returner gets schemes
  that funnel to the middle and let him make people miss).
- **The hidden weapon**: A great return specialist changes field position math
  for the entire game. If your opponent knows a touchback gives you the ball at
  the 25, but a kick gives the returner a chance to flip the field, they're
  making different decisions. That ripple effect is real and undervalued.

**Gunner:** The kamikaze missile on punt coverage. Sprints down the field,
avoids or defeats two blockers, and makes the tackle on the returner. The gunner
is pure special teams — his value is entirely in his ability to cover kicks.

- **Primary attributes**: Speed, tackling, acceleration, agility, durability
- **Secondary attributes**: Strength (defeating jams at the line), composure
  (making tackles in space at full speed)
- **Physical profile**: 5'10"-6'2", 190-210 lbs; typically a WR or DB body type
  with exceptional speed and fearlessness
- **Scheme fit**: Every punt team needs gunners, but the question is whether you
  carry a player whose primary value is gunner duty. Elite gunners are often WRs
  or DBs who can contribute on offense or defense — Matthew Slater is the
  archetype of a player who carved out a Hall of Fame career almost entirely on
  special teams. A great STC maximizes gunner talent; a bad STC wastes it.
- **The roster puzzle**: The gunner epitomizes the 53-man roster game. Do you
  keep the 5th corner who's an elite gunner, or the 5th corner who's a better
  defensive player but a liability on coverage teams? That decision is the
  difference between a punt return touchdown against you and a punt downed at
  the 8-yard line. Special teams coaches live and die by these roster fringe
  decisions.

**Kick/punt coverage teams (the unsung units):** Beyond the gunner, the entire
coverage unit matters. Coverage teams are built from players across the roster —
linebackers, safeties, running backs, tight ends — who double as special
teamers. A player's special teams value is a hidden factor in roster
construction:

- A backup linebacker who's an elite coverage player might make the roster over
  a more talented LB who's a liability on special teams
- STCs identify and develop special teams contributors from across the roster
- Teams that neglect special teams personnel give up return touchdowns and lose
  field position — which loses close games
- The "core four" special teamers (players who play on multiple ST units) are
  among the most valuable depth players on the roster, even if they never start
  on offense or defense

## Personnel & Play Calls

Archetypes describe who a player is. Personnel groupings and positional labels
describe how the coach deploys those players on a given snap. A coach's
preferences here — which groupings he lives in, how he uses each label, how
often he motions, how he matches up against defensive personnel — are part of
his scheme fingerprint, and they shape what your roster actually needs beyond
raw archetype fit.

### Offensive Personnel Groupings

Personnel groupings are labeled with a two-digit number. The first digit is the
number of running backs on the field, the second is the number of tight ends.
Everyone else (minus the five offensive linemen and the quarterback) is a
receiver. So 11 personnel = 1 RB + 1 TE + 3 WR. 21 personnel = 2 RB + 1 TE + 2
WR. The grouping doesn't say anything about formation — an offense can line up
11 personnel in shotgun trips, empty, under-center play-action, or ten other
looks — but it does tell the defense (and your roster) what skill positions must
be on the field.

**11 personnel (1 RB / 1 TE / 3 WR):** The modern NFL's default grouping. Most
teams spend 50-70% of their offensive snaps here. Demands a tight end who can
both block and threaten as a receiver (a one-dimensional TE tips the play) and a
legitimate every-down slot receiver — not a gadget piece, a starter. An offense
that lives in 11 personnel but has a blocking-only Y has a problem: defenses
will go nickel and dare you to run.

**12 personnel (1 RB / 2 TE / 2 WR):** The play-action grouping. Two tight ends
— typically one inline Y blocker and one move F/H-back — creates a heavy look
that defenses have to honor against the run, which is what makes the play-action
pass lethal. The real value of 12 personnel is that it forces the defense to
make a choice: stay in base and you're outmatched by a 3x1 spread formation; go
nickel and you're light against the run. Requires carrying two credible TEs on
the roster, which is a real investment.

**13 personnel (1 RB / 3 TE / 1 WR):** Heavy/goal-line grouping. Only works if
you've drafted and developed TE depth well past where most teams invest. The
payoff: near-unsolvable short-yardage looks, strong play-action in the red zone,
and formation flexibility where any of the three TEs can flex out. The cost: a
roster spot philosophy most teams can't afford.

**21 personnel (2 RB / 1 TE / 2 WR):** Classic power/lead grouping. Requires a
fullback — a roster spot most teams won't spend on. A coach who uses 21
personnel meaningfully is making a philosophical statement about how he wants to
play offense: downhill, physical, play-action off run action. In a spread
league, 21 personnel is a throwback — but one that can punish a defense built to
defend spread looks.

**22 personnel (2 RB / 2 TE / 1 WR):** Jumbo / short-yardage identity. Eight
blockers plus the QB. Used primarily in short yardage, goal line, and
four-minute offense. A coach with a strong 22 package has a clear identity when
games get physical — and a real answer on 3rd-and-1 that doesn't depend on his
QB's legs.

**10 personnel (1 RB / 0 TE / 4 WR):** Spread grouping. Four wide receivers
forces the defense into dime or a light nickel, which can open up running lanes
for a creative OC willing to run from spread. Requires WR depth and a back who
can block in pass protection without tight end help.

**Empty (0 RB / any TE count, 5 receivers wide):** Not technically a personnel
package — it's a formation alignment out of existing personnel — but coaches
treat empty tendencies as a distinct fingerprint. Heavy empty usage forces the
QB to handle protection calls alone and commits to throwing the ball.

#### Personnel lean as a coaching fingerprint

A coach's distribution across these groupings is a hidden tendency. Two OCs both
called "modern" might split very differently:

```
OC A — Spread-leaning
  11 personnel: 72%     12 personnel: 14%     21/22: 2%
  10/empty:     12%

OC B — Multiple pro-style
  11 personnel: 48%     12 personnel: 31%     21 personnel: 11%
  13 personnel: 6%      10/empty:      4%
```

Same league, same era, completely different roster requirements. OC A can get by
with one TE and needs four roster-worthy receivers. OC B needs two starting TEs,
a fullback or H-back, and can play three-receiver football with less depth
behind his top two.

#### Personnel vs. defensive personnel

Personnel is a matchup game. When the offense substitutes, the defense
substitutes back. This creates leverage points a good OC exploits:

- **No-huddle locks the defense in.** If you snap the ball before the defense
  can substitute, whatever package they were in is what they're stuck with. A
  spread offense that goes no-huddle after a 12-personnel run is a classic way
  to catch base defense on the field against a 3x1 passing look.
- **12 personnel forces a defensive choice.** Stay in base (4 DB, 3 LB) and the
  offense has a physical run advantage with two TEs; go nickel and the offense
  spreads out and has a body-count advantage in the run game with a DB on the
  field instead of an LB. Either way, the OC wins something.
- **Heavy personnel against sub-packages is a gift.** If the defense stays in
  nickel against 22 personnel out of ego or scheme rigidity, the offense runs
  the ball at a light box all day. An adaptable DC will match personnel; a
  stubborn one won't, and the offense collects.
- **Light personnel against base is the mirror image.** 10 personnel against a
  base defense that refuses to sub is a coverage advantage the offense should
  attack immediately, and a spread-capable OC will.

### Offensive Positional Labels

Personnel tells you how many of each position group are on the field. Labels
tell you what role each of them is playing on this specific snap. Labels are
positional responsibilities, not player identities — motion and formation shifts
rotate them, and some offenses flip labels by strength while others keep them
fixed by side.

**X (split end / boundary receiver):** On the line of scrimmage, typically to
the short side of the field (boundary). Rarely motions because he's covering the
LOS on his side. Usually the offense's alpha — the guy who wins contested
catches in single coverage on the backside of a 3x1 formation. Maps to the **X
receiver archetype** from the archetypes section.

**Z (flanker / field receiver):** Off the line of scrimmage (because the Y is on
the LOS on his side), typically to the wide side of the field. Motion- eligible
— a Z who doesn't motion is a waste of the position, because the whole point of
Z is using pre-snap movement to diagnose coverage and create leverage. Maps to
the **Z receiver archetype**.

**Y (inline tight end):** On the line of scrimmage, attached to the formation.
The Y's skill set drives his usage: a blocking-Y sets the edge in the run game
and chips pass rushers; a receiving-Y is the seam-stretching, coverage- warping
mismatch the archetypes section describes. Most offenses need a credible Y who
can do both at a baseline level — a pure blocking Y in 11 personnel tips the
play 60% of the time.

**F / H (move tight end / H-back):** Off the line of scrimmage. Aligns anywhere
— offset in the backfield, in the slot, wing off the tackle, split out wide. The
F/H is the scheme's chess piece: motion him, and his alignment post-motion tells
you what the offense wants. Maps to the **H-back / move TE archetype**. In 12
personnel, the F is usually the more athletic of the two TEs; in 21 personnel,
the F role is often filled by a fullback or a hybrid.

**Slot (interior receiver in 3+ WR sets):** In 11 personnel, the slot is the
third receiver, aligned between the tackle and the outside WR. In 10 personnel,
there are two slots. Slot is a distinct role with its own archetype (the slot
receiver archetype) — not just "the third-best WR." A slot works in traffic,
runs option routes, and catches in the high-contact middle of the field. Teams
that use a boundary corner's backup at slot give up a structural matchup on 60%+
of snaps.

**How personnel dictates labels on the field:**

```
11 personnel:  X  +  Z  +  Slot  +  Y  +  RB
12 personnel:  X  +  Z  +  Y  +  F (H-back)  +  RB
13 personnel:  X  +  Y1  +  Y2  +  F  +  RB
21 personnel:  X  +  Z  +  Y  +  FB  +  RB
10 personnel:  X  +  Z  +  Slot1  +  Slot2  +  RB
```

Motion rotates these labels. An offense that motions the Z into the slot
pre-snap is functionally playing a different formation than where it lined up,
and the label that matters for the play is the post-motion alignment. A coach's
motion rate is itself a tendency — high-motion offenses (Shanahan- tree,
McVay-tree, McDaniel) use motion diagnostically on 60%+ of snaps; low-motion
offenses run the same formation static and win with execution.

**Label rules vs. flipping:** Some offenses assign labels by role and flip them
based on formation strength — X is always the backside boundary receiver, so he
switches sides when the formation flips. Other offenses keep labels fixed by
player — X is always #11, lined up wherever he lines up. This is a scheme detail
that affects how versatile your receivers need to be. A fixed-label offense can
carry a one-sided receiver; a strength-flipping offense needs receivers who can
execute the same route tree from either side.

## Scheme Fit

Scheme fit is not a separate rating. It's **emergent from the interaction
between a player's attributes and the demands of the coach's tendencies.**

There is no hidden "scheme fit: 72" on any player. Instead, the coach's
tendencies define which attributes matter most, and the player either has those
attributes or he doesn't. The degree of alignment IS the fit.

### How it works

A West Coast OC's system weights short accuracy, touch, decision-making, and
clutch heavily. A QB with 85 short accuracy, 80 touch, and 75 decision-making
will perform well in this system. The same QB in an Air Raid system — which
weights quick release, deep accuracy, and arm strength more heavily — might
perform worse because the system is asking for attributes he doesn't have at the
same level.

The inverse is equally true. A cannon-armed gunslinger with 90 arm strength and
60 short accuracy will be exposed in a West Coast system that asks him to make
47 short timing throws per game. Put him in a vertical passing offense that asks
him to throw 8 deep shots per game, and his arm strength becomes the defining
weapon.

**Nothing about the player changed. The context changed.**

### The layers of fit

Scheme fit operates at multiple levels:

1. **Tendency fit** — does the player's attribute profile align with what the
   coach's tendencies demand? A speed rusher fits a four-man-rush DC's scheme. A
   run-stuffing nose tackle fits a two-gap DC's scheme.

2. **Archetype fit** — does the player match the archetype the scheme needs at
   his position? The coach needs a zone runner; your back is a power runner.
   He'll underperform because the scheme is asking him to read and cut, but his
   skill is in pressing the hole and running through tackles.

3. **Complementary fit** — does the player complement the other players in the
   scheme? An elite X receiver paired with a deep-threat Z and a reliable slot
   gives the offense three distinct weapons. Three players who all run the same
   routes create redundancy that the defense can exploit.

4. **System familiarity** — has the player been in this type of system before? A
   veteran corner who's played man coverage his entire career will adapt to a
   new man-heavy scheme faster than one who's been in zone his whole career.
   This is a temporal factor — familiarity improves over time as the player
   learns the system.

### Scheme fit and player value

This is where scheme fit intersects with the market and creates inefficiencies:

- A player who's a **poor fit for his current team** might be underperforming
  his true ability. If you can acquire him and put him in a better scheme fit,
  you've found a market inefficiency. This is the trade acquisition that looks
  confusing on paper but makes perfect sense once you see him in your system.
- A player who's a **great fit for his current team** might be overperforming
  relative to his raw attributes. If he hits free agency and joins a team with a
  different scheme, he might regress. This is the free agency signing that looks
  great on paper and disappoints immediately.
- A draft prospect's scheme fit depends on **the coach you haven't hired yet** —
  or the coach you might fire next year. Drafting a 3-4 nose tackle when your DC
  runs a 3-4 is smart. It's less smart if you fire that DC next offseason and
  hire a 4-3 guy.

### What the user sees

You never see a scheme fit number. What you see:

- **Performance** — a player performing below expectations might be a scheme fit
  issue, a coaching issue, or a talent issue. You diagnose it the same way a
  real GM does: by watching, studying, and consulting your staff.
- **Coach's depth chart** — if your coach is benching a player you think is
  talented, maybe the coach sees a scheme fit problem you don't.
- **Stats in context** — a running back averaging 3.2 YPC in a zone scheme might
  average 4.5 in a gap scheme. The stats alone don't tell you why. Your job is
  to figure it out.
- **Scout reports** — when evaluating a trade target or free agent, your scouts
  assess scheme fit based on their understanding of your coach's system. But
  scouts can be wrong about scheme fit — especially if the scout doesn't fully
  understand the coach's tendencies.

## How Schemes Interact with Other Systems

### Scouting and Drafting

Your coach's tendencies determine your draft board. Two GMs can look at the same
edge rusher and see completely different values:

```
Edge Rusher Prospect — "Jordan Ellis"
  Speed: 92  Strength: 71  Pass Rush Moves: 85  Run Defense: 65

  DC with speed-rush, four-man-rush tendencies:
    ELITE prospect — his speed and pass rush ability are exactly what
    this defense needs off the edge

  DC with two-gap, run-first tendencies:
    POOR fit — not strong enough to set the edge in a system that
    asks the DE to control the C-gap against the run
```

This means positional scarcity is scheme-dependent. A "weak" draft class for one
team might be deep for another.

### Free Agency

Players on the market have scheme histories. A corner who thrived in a Cover 3
system might struggle transitioning to man-heavy press coverage. This is a
factor in both the player's decision and your evaluation.

### Coaching Staff

Coaches bring their tendencies with them. Hiring decisions cascade:

- You hire an OC from the Air Raid tree
- Your roster was built for power run — big linemen, a bruiser back, a blocking
  tight end
- Now you're facing a multi-year transition: draft speed players, release power
  players, or accept a mismatch and underperform while you retool
- Meanwhile, your existing players' development is disrupted — a power- blocking
  guard isn't suddenly going to become a zone-scheme athlete

See [Coaches](./coaches.md) for the full coaching system.

### Game Simulation

Tendency vs. tendency matchups influence outcomes. This isn't rock-paper-
scissors — talent still matters most — but schematic edges are real:

- A spread offense with four WRs against a base defense that won't go to nickel
  creates mismatches
- A power run team in bad weather against a finesse defense has an edge
- A zone blitz can rattle a young QB but a veteran reads it and audibles
- Halftime adjustments from good coordinators can swing a game
- A coach whose tendencies have become predictable gets exploited by opponents
  who study the tape; this is the "figured out by week 8" phenomenon

### Scheme Transitions

Changing coaches means changing tendencies, and changing tendencies is costly:

- Players lose their scheme fit alignment during transition (1-2 season
  adjustment)
- Some players may never fit the new tendencies and need to be moved
- Young players adapt faster than veterans
- Coaching staff continuity accelerates the transition
- The payoff is worth it if the new coaching staff better fits your long-term
  roster vision

This is why firing a coach is never just about the coach — it's about the
cascade. A new DC who runs a Cover 3 inherits a roster built for Cover 2. Your
$15M free safety you signed for his range is now a box safety in a scheme that
needs a different skill set. The coaching hire decision IS the scheme decision,
and both are roster decisions.

This creates one of the game's deepest strategic questions: do you hire coaches
who fit your current players, or draft players who fit the coaches you want?
