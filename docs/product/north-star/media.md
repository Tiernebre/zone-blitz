# Media

The media is the voice of the league. It reacts to everything you do — and
everything you don't do. It shapes narratives, applies pressure, influences
player perception, and creates the ambient texture that makes a franchise feel
like it exists in a living world rather than a spreadsheet.

You don't interact with the media. You observe it. Headlines scroll by. Analysts
argue. Fans react. And all of it — the praise, the criticism, the hot takes —
has consequences you have to live with.

---

## Core Design Principle: Media as a Mirror with a Megaphone

The media reflects what's happening in the league, but it amplifies and
distorts. A quiet 7-9 season gets a shrug. A 7-9 season after trading three
first-round picks for a quarterback gets a firestorm. Context matters, and the
media remembers context.

The media is not omniscient. It works with publicly available information —
standings, stats, transactions, contract details, draft picks. It doesn't know
your scouting reports or your private board. But it has opinions about
everything, and those opinions affect the world around you.

---

## Media Personalities

The media ecosystem is populated by generated personalities — analysts,
reporters, and pundits — each with their own tendencies, beats, and track
records. These aren't interchangeable voices. Over time, you learn who's
insightful and who's noise, the same way you learn which scouts to trust.

### General analysts

The broadest category. These are the talking heads who cover the entire league
and react to everything — trades, free agency, coaching hires, weekly results,
playoff races.

Each general analyst has tendencies:

- **The hot take artist**: Overreacts to everything. A two-game losing streak is
  a "franchise in crisis." A mid-round rookie who has a good debut is "the steal
  of the draft." Entertaining, occasionally right, usually not.
- **The measured voice**: Cautious, waits for sample sizes, hedges predictions.
  Less exciting to read but more likely to be correct over time. When this
  analyst says something is a problem, it probably is.
- **The old-school traditionalist**: Values running the ball, drafting in the
  trenches, and "building through the draft." Skeptical of analytics, spread
  offenses, and any GM who trades up for a skill player in round one.
- **The analytics evangelist**: Loves efficiency metrics, expected points added,
  and contract value models. Dismisses eye-test narratives. His analysis is
  often rigorous but occasionally misses the human element that makes football
  unpredictable.
- **The narrative builder**: Sees storylines everywhere. Connects this year's
  coaching hire to last year's playoff loss to the draft pick three years ago.
  He turns your franchise into a saga — sometimes insightfully, sometimes by
  forcing connections that aren't there.

### Draft specialists

These are the Mel Kipers — analysts who live and breathe the draft cycle. They
appear prominently in the pre-draft period and fade into the background during
the regular season.

Draft specialists are already described in the
[Scouting — Media Mock Drafts](./scouting.md#media-mock-drafts) section: the
consensus builder, the contrarian, the insider, the analytics guy, the beat
reporter. This document doesn't duplicate that — draft specialists are part of
the broader media ecosystem, and their mock drafts, prospect rankings, and
draft-day commentary feed into the same media layer as everything else.

What this document adds: draft specialists also publish **draft grades** after
the event (see [Draft Grades](#draft-grades)) and their grades carry weight in
shaping public narrative around your team.

### Cap and contract analysts

Specialists who track the salary cap, contract structures, and financial
implications of every move:

- They project cap space for every team before free agency
- They grade free agent signings based on contract value vs. projected
  production
- They flag teams heading for cap trouble ("The Jets have $3M in cap space and
  14 free agents next year — this is going to get ugly")
- They evaluate restructures and cap maneuvers — praising savvy moves and
  calling out teams mortgaging the future

These analysts are useful because their contract analysis is often more
objective than narrative-driven coverage. When a cap analyst says you overpaid,
the market probably agrees.

### Coaching pundits

Analysts who focus on scheme, coaching performance, and the coaching carousel:

- They evaluate coaching hires and firings, with takes that age well or poorly
- They break down scheme matchups during the season ("This defense is built to
  stop the run but gets shredded by spread formations — and that's exactly what
  they'll face on Sunday")
- They track the coaching hot seat and predict firings before they happen
- They identify rising coordinator candidates and predict coaching carousel
  movement

### Beat reporters

Each team has beat reporters who cover that franchise specifically:

- They surface team-specific news: depth chart battles, practice reports, injury
  updates, locker room atmosphere
- They sometimes report on internal friction — a player unhappy with his role, a
  coach frustrated with personnel decisions — based on "sources"
- Beat reporter quality varies. Some are well-connected and surface real signal.
  Others fill column inches with speculation. You learn which is which over
  time.
- Beat reporters for _other_ teams are useful too — if another team's beat
  reporter says their GM is looking to trade down in the draft, that's
  actionable intelligence (if you trust the reporter)

### Personality persistence

Media personalities persist across seasons. A draft analyst who gave your 2042
class an F has a track record you can check in 2045. An analyst who predicted
your rebuild would fail has to eat those words — or gets vindicated — when the
results come in.

This persistence matters because it makes the media feel like part of the world,
not procedurally generated noise. You develop opinions about specific analysts
the same way you develop opinions about specific scouts.

## What the Media Covers

### Headlines and news feed

The primary media surface is a **league news feed** — a stream of headlines,
short articles, and reactions that updates throughout the season and offseason.
This is the heartbeat of the league.

Headlines are generated for:

- **Transactions**: Every trade, signing, cut, extension, and draft pick
  generates coverage. Bigger moves get bigger coverage. A blockbuster trade
  generates multiple analyst reactions; a minor depth signing gets a one-line
  note.
- **Game results**: Upsets, blowouts, playoff implications, milestone
  performances. "Eagles stunned at home by 3-win Giants" gets a headline. A
  routine win over a bad team might not.
- **Injuries**: Significant injuries to key players generate coverage and
  analysis of the impact.
- **Coaching changes**: Hires, firings, and coordinator moves.
- **Milestones**: Player records, career achievements, franchise records.
- **Rumors and speculation**: "Sources say the Bears are exploring the
  quarterback market" — may or may not be accurate, but it's out there.
- **Off-field events**: Holdouts, contract disputes, franchise tag reactions.

Not everything generates a headline. The feed is curated to surface what matters
and skip the mundane. A Tuesday practice report doesn't make the league news —
but a star player missing practice during a contract dispute does.

### Power rankings

Published weekly during the season. Every general analyst publishes their own
rankings, and a consensus ranking aggregates them.

Power rankings are:

- **Subjective and inconsistent** — different analysts weigh wins, point
  differential, eye test, and strength of schedule differently
- **Reactive** — a team that wins three straight climbs; a team that loses to a
  bad opponent drops. Analysts overreact to recent results, just like real power
  rankings.
- **Narrative-driven** — a team with a compelling story ("incredible turnaround
  after 2-5 start") gets a boost that the numbers alone might not justify
- **Debatable** — seeing your team ranked 18th when you think you're a top-10
  team is the kind of low-stakes friction that makes the world feel alive

Power rankings have no direct mechanical effect, but they contribute to the
ambient perception of your franchise — and they feed into other media
narratives.

### Weekly reactions and game coverage

After each week of games, analysts react:

- **Game recaps** with analysis of key performances, turning points, and
  coaching decisions
- **Player of the week** and **performance highlights** — both positive ("Rookie
  QB throws 4 TDs in debut") and negative ("$80M pass rusher held without a sack
  for the 5th straight game")
- **Coaching critique**: Analysts second-guess play calls, fourth-down
  decisions, clock management, and personnel usage. Sometimes they're right.
  Sometimes they're armchair quarterbacks.
- **Trend pieces**: "The Vikings have lost 4 straight — is the window closing?"
  or "The Jaguars' defense hasn't allowed a rushing TD in 6 weeks — here's why"

### Trade reactions

Every trade gets graded by multiple analysts. Grades include:

- **Immediate letter grade** (A+ through F) for each side of the deal
- **Brief analysis** explaining the reasoning — was it fair value? Did one side
  fleece the other? Does it make sense for both teams' windows?
- **Analyst disagreement** — a trade one analyst calls an A might get a C from
  another. The spread of grades tells you how the market perceives the deal.
- **Context-aware analysis**: Analysts consider each team's situation. The same
  trade can be graded differently for a contender and a rebuilder.

Trade grades are archived and re-evaluated over time (see
[Revisiting Old Takes](#revisiting-old-takes)).

### Free agency grades

When teams make significant signings, analysts react:

- **Signing grade**: Letter grade for the deal — did the team overpay? Underpay?
  Was it a good fit?
- **Cap implications**: Cap analysts specifically evaluate the contract
  structure — front-loaded, back-loaded, void years, guaranteed money. "Great
  player, terrible contract" is a real grade.
- **Overall free agency class grade**: After the initial free agency wave,
  analysts grade each team's entire haul. Who won free agency? Who overspent?
  Who was suspiciously quiet?

### Draft grades

Draft grades are a significant media event, already described in
[Drafting — Draft Grades](./drafting.md#draft-grades). The media document adds
the broader context: draft grades are one piece of the media's year-round
grading of your decisions. The same analyst who gave your draft an A- and your
free agency a C+ is building a running narrative about your front office.

### Coaching hire and fire reactions

When a coach is hired or fired, the media reacts:

- **Firing reactions**: Was it justified? Did the coach get a fair shake? Was it
  the coach's fault or the GM's roster? Analysts weigh in with varying degrees
  of sympathy.
- **Hire grades**: Letter grades and analysis. "This is an uninspired hire —
  recycling a failed head coach because he's safe" vs. "Bold choice going with
  the young offensive coordinator — high ceiling if he can manage a staff."
- **Coordinator hire analysis**: Lower profile but still covered. "New DC runs a
  3-4 — how does that fit the personnel on the roster?"

### Preseason and offseason coverage

The offseason isn't dead air:

- **Offseason power rankings / tier lists**: Where does each team stand heading
  into the new year?
- **Offseason grades**: An aggregate assessment of each team's total offseason —
  draft + free agency + trades + coaching changes. "The Dolphins upgraded at
  every level. A+ offseason." or "The Cowboys stood pat while the division got
  better. Risky."
- **Award predictions**: Preseason MVP, Rookie of the Year, Coach of the Year
  predictions. These age well or poorly and get revisited.
- **Over/under win totals**: Projected win totals for each team. A fun benchmark
  to track throughout the season.
- **Playoff predictions**: Analysts pick their playoff brackets, division
  winners, and Super Bowl picks before the season starts.

### Award races and MVP watch

During the season, analysts track award races:

- **MVP watch**: Weekly updated rankings of MVP candidates with analysis
- **Rookie of the Year**: Tracking which first-year players are making an impact
- **Coach of the Year**: Usually whoever is most exceeding expectations
- **Comeback Player of the Year**: Players returning from injury or decline
- **Defensive Player of the Year**: The best defensive performers

These are narrative drivers. A player on your team being in the MVP conversation
generates positive coverage. A highly-paid player conspicuously absent from any
award discussion generates questions.

## Revisiting Old Takes

One of the media system's most distinctive features: **analysts are held
accountable by the passage of time**. Every graded take is archived and
re-evaluated as reality unfolds.

### What gets revisited

- **Trade grades**: Re-graded after 1 year and 3 years based on how the traded
  players and picks performed
- **Free agency signing grades**: Re-graded as contracts play out — was the
  player worth the money? Did the contract structure age well?
- **Draft grades**: Re-graded after 1 year, 3 years, and 5 years (as described
  in the [Drafting](./drafting.md#draft-grades) document)
- **Coaching hire grades**: Re-graded based on the coach's actual results
- **Preseason predictions**: Win totals, playoff picks, and award predictions
  are compared against actual results at season's end
- **GM and front office assessments**: The aggregate narrative about a front
  office evolves as decisions play out. An analyst who called your rebuild
  "directionless" two years ago might be writing about your "masterclass in
  patience" now — or doubling down.

### The re-grade experience

When you view a past transaction or event, you can see:

- **The original take**: What analysts said at the time, preserved exactly
- **The re-grade**: What analysts say now, with the benefit of hindsight
- **Side-by-side comparison**: The original grade next to the updated grade,
  with commentary on what changed
- **Analyst track record**: Over many re-grades, you can see which analysts are
  consistently prescient and which are consistently wrong. This is the same
  "evaluate the evaluator" dynamic that drives the scouting system — applied to
  media.

### Why this matters

Re-grades create a **long-tail payoff for every decision**. A trade you made
three years ago gets revisited. A draft pick you sweated over gets a verdict.
The analyst who clowned your second-round pick has to acknowledge the player is
now All-Pro — or the analyst who praised it gets to say "told you so."

This also creates institutional memory for the league. In year 8 of a franchise,
you can look back at year 2 and trace the narrative arc — how the media
perceived your rebuild in real time vs. how it actually played out. That's
storytelling depth that most sports sims don't attempt.

## Media's Mechanical Effects

The media isn't just flavor. Coverage has real consequences that affect gameplay
systems.

### Player morale and perception

Media narratives affect how players feel about their situation:

- **Negative coverage of a player** ("$80M pass rusher is invisible") can damage
  that player's morale, especially if the player has a media-sensitive
  personality. Some players tune it out; others internalize the criticism.
- **Positive coverage** can boost confidence — a player named to a midseason
  award watch or praised as a breakout performer gets a morale lift.
- **"Sources say" reports** about a player being unhappy, being shopped in
  trades, or being on the trading block affect that player's relationship with
  the team — even if you never actually shopped him. Leaks happen (from coaches,
  agents, or other front offices), and the media amplifies them.
- **Contract narrative pressure**: When a media consensus forms that a player
  "deserves" a new deal or is "underpaid," it can increase that player's
  contract expectations and decrease their patience during negotiations.

### Free agent perception

Media coverage shapes how free agents perceive your organization:

- **Positive narrative**: Teams with good media coverage — praised for culture,
  smart management, competitive roster — are more attractive to free agents. A
  free agent choosing between two similar offers may lean toward the team the
  media portrays as "on the rise."
- **Negative narrative**: Teams savaged in the press — called dysfunctional,
  directionless, or cheap — are less attractive. Free agents read the headlines.
  A team mocked as "cap hell with no plan" has to overpay to attract talent.
- **Coaching perception**: Media coverage of your coaching staff affects free
  agent decisions. A coach praised as a "quarterback whisperer" or "defensive
  guru" can be a draw. A coach the media calls a "dead man walking" is a
  deterrent.

### Fan and league-peer sentiment

Zone Blitz has no opaque owner layer pressuring you from above — every franchise
is run by a fused owner/GM (see [League Setup](./league-setup.md)). Media
coverage instead shapes **fan and league-peer sentiment**: the pressure you feel
from the base you answer to (fans, city, sponsors) and from the other owner/GMs
you share the league with.

- Sustained negative coverage — losing seasons, criticized transactions,
  "dysfunction" narratives — erodes fan engagement and your standing among
  peers. It doesn't get you fired (you can't fire yourself), but it affects free
  agent appeal, expansion-vote influence, and the willingness of coaches and
  scouts to join you
- Positive coverage — praised moves, playoff runs, "turnaround" narratives —
  builds the opposite: fan loyalty, peer respect, and recruiting gravity
- Coverage is read by everyone at the table. When every analyst says the coach
  needs to be fired, every other owner/GM in the league sees it too, and that
  shapes how they negotiate with you, vote alongside you, and pursue hires
  against you.

### Draft and trade leverage

Media narratives subtly affect your leverage in deals:

- If the media consensus is that a player you're trying to trade is washed,
  other GMs have ammunition to lowball you — "everyone knows he's declining"
- If the media is hyping a player on your roster, his perceived value increases
  — other GMs may offer more in a trade to acquire "the breakout star of the
  season"
- Pre-draft media buzz about which players your team is targeting can tip off
  rival GMs who want the same player — creating trade-up pressure you wouldn't
  have faced if your interest were private

## Social Media Layer

Beneath the analyst and reporter layer, there's a vague, ambient **social media
presence** that represents the fan base and broader public reaction.

### What social media adds

Social media is not detailed analysis. It's emotional, reactive, and loud:

- **Fan sentiment**: A general mood indicator for your team's fan base —
  excited, frustrated, apathetic, furious. This shifts with wins, losses,
  transactions, and narratives.
- **Viral moments**: A spectacular play, a bizarre coaching decision, or a
  controversial trade might "go viral" — generating outsized attention that
  doesn't match the actual significance. A backup quarterback throwing a
  highlight-reel touchdown in garbage time shouldn't change your evaluation of
  him, but the fan base is suddenly demanding he start.
- **Overreaction amplification**: Social media amplifies the hottest takes. If
  an analyst says something provocative about your team, social media makes it
  louder. A measured critique becomes "FIRE THE GM" trending.
- **Memes and narratives**: Certain events become running jokes or narratives
  that follow your franchise. A bad draft pick becomes a meme. A surprising
  success becomes a feel-good story. These narratives have a half-life but can
  linger for years.

### What social media doesn't do

- It doesn't provide useful analysis — that's what the professional media layer
  is for
- It doesn't have individual personalities you track — it's a collective vibe,
  not specific people
- It doesn't have direct mechanical effects beyond contributing to the ambient
  perception of your franchise (which feeds into free agent attractiveness and
  owner patience)

Social media is atmosphere. It makes the world feel noisier and more alive, the
way a stadium crowd makes a game feel real even though the crowd isn't playing.

## What Makes Media Fun

The media is fun because it makes your decisions feel **consequential beyond the
box score**. A trade isn't just a roster move — it's a public event that gets
graded, debated, and remembered. A bad season isn't just a bad record — it's a
narrative about your franchise that follows you into the next year.

The best media moments:

- "Every analyst gave my trade a D. Three years later, the re-grade is an A. I
  was right and they were all wrong. Receipts."
- "The consensus had my offseason as the worst in the league. We went 12-5. I
  want to frame that power ranking from August."
- "A beat reporter leaked that I was shopping my franchise quarterback. I
  wasn't. Now the QB is unhappy and his agent is demanding a trade. The media
  created this crisis."
- "The hot take artist has been predicting my coach will be fired for three
  straight years. The coach just won Coach of the Year. I wonder what the take
  is now."
- "My team has been ranked last in the preseason power rankings for two straight
  years. The fans are restless. But I know the rebuild is almost done — next
  year's draft class is stacked and I have four picks in the top 50."
- "The cap analyst called my restructure 'reckless.' He might be right. I've got
  two years before the bill comes due. If we win a championship in that window,
  it was genius. If we don't, it was exactly as reckless as he said."
- "Social media is losing its mind because I drafted a punter in the fourth
  round. They'll understand when he's pinning teams inside the 10 every week. Or
  they won't. Either way, the memes are pretty funny."
