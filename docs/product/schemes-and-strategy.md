# Schemes & Strategy

Football schemes are the connective tissue of the entire game. Your team's
scheme defines what kind of players you need, which prospects you value, which
free agents you target, and how your team performs on the field. But you don't
pick a scheme from a menu — **your coaches determine your scheme**. You want to
run a 3-4 defense? Hire a DC who runs a 3-4. You want a spread offense? Hire an
OC who installs one. Your scheme decision is a coaching hire decision, and
changing your scheme means changing your coaches — which ripples through every
other system.

## Why Schemes Matter

In a game without schemes, team-building is simple: get the highest-rated
players at every position. Schemes make it a puzzle. A player with elite
attributes who doesn't fit your scheme might perform worse than a player with
good-not-great attributes who fits perfectly. This means:

- Two GMs looking at the same draft class build completely different boards
- A player's trade value depends on who's buying
- Coaching hires have multi-year roster implications
- There's no single "correct" way to build a team

## Schemes Are Tendencies, Not Labels

The modern NFL doesn't run pure schemes. Nobody is a "pure 3-4 team" or a
"pure West Coast offense" anymore. The distinction between a 4-3 defensive
end and a 3-4 outside linebacker has blurred into "edge rusher." Most teams
play nickel as their base defense. Offenses blend concepts from multiple
traditions on every drive.

**In this game, a coach doesn't select a scheme from a dropdown.** A coach
brings a **set of tendencies, principles, and preferences** that define how
his unit plays football. The "scheme" is the aggregate of these tendencies —
a fingerprint, not a label.

### Offensive tendencies

An offensive coordinator's identity is defined by where he falls on several
spectrums:

**Run/pass philosophy:**
- Run-heavy ←→ Pass-heavy — the fundamental orientation; a run-heavy OC might
  target 55-60% run plays; a pass-heavy OC might throw on 65%+ of plays
- Early-down run tendency — some OCs establish the run on 1st and 2nd down
  and throw on 3rd; others spread you out from the first snap

**Personnel philosophy:**
- Light ←→ Heavy — does the OC prefer 11 personnel (1 RB, 1 TE, 3 WR) or
  12/21/22 personnel (multiple TEs and/or RBs)? This determines which
  position groups matter most for your roster.
- TE usage — is the tight end a glorified offensive lineman, a primary
  receiving weapon, or a versatile H-back who does everything? This single
  tendency can make or break a TE's career on your team.
- RB usage — workhorse back who gets 25 carries, committee approach with
  specialized roles, or receiving back who's basically a slot receiver?

**Tempo:**
- Slow and methodical ←→ Up-tempo / no-huddle — affects play volume, fatigue,
  time of possession, and the kind of QB you need (a no-huddle QB must
  process fast and manage the line of scrimmage himself)

**Formation philosophy:**
- Under center ←→ Shotgun/pistol — affects run game style, play-action
  effectiveness, and QB skill set requirements
- Formation diversity — does the OC line up in 15 different formations to
  create confusion, or does he run the same 3-4 looks and execute perfectly?
  Diverse formations stress the defense but require smarter, more versatile
  players.
- Pre-snap motion — heavy motion (Shanahan-tree, McVay-tree) creates
  diagnostic information and mismatches but adds complexity

**Passing philosophy:**
- Timing-based ←→ Improvisation-based — a timing offense needs precision and
  rhythm (West Coast, Air Raid); an improvisation-based offense needs a QB
  who can extend plays and receivers who can win off-script
- Short/intermediate ←→ Vertical — does the OC attack underneath and let
  receivers run after the catch, or push the ball downfield and take shots?
- Progression reads ←→ Simplified reads — a full-field progression offense
  (pro-style) requires elite processing; a half-field or single-read offense
  (RPO, Air Raid) simplifies the QB's job but limits options

**Run game philosophy:**
- Zone ←→ Gap/power — the fundamental blocking scheme distinction; zone
  schemes want athletic, mobile linemen who can reach and climb; gap/power
  schemes want big, strong linemen who can drive defenders off the ball
- Inside ←→ Outside — does the run game attack between the tackles or on the
  perimeter? Inside zone, outside zone, power, counter, stretch — each
  demands different OL and RB attributes
- RPO integration — how heavily does the OC attach run-pass options to the
  run game? Heavy RPO usage requires a QB with elite processing and an OL
  that can handle the ambiguity of blocking for both run and pass

### Defensive tendencies

A defensive coordinator's identity is similarly defined by spectrums:

**Front philosophy:**
- Odd ←→ Even front — three down linemen (odd) vs. four (even); but in
  modern football, the distinction is blurry because nickel packages change
  the math on every passing down
- One-gap ←→ Two-gap — do your linemen attack a single gap (penetration,
  disruption) or control two gaps (space-eating, discipline)? This is the
  most important DL personnel distinction in the game.
- Edge rush philosophy — speed rush off the edge, power rush, or versatile
  rushers who can do both? A DC who wants speed rushers needs a very
  different edge player than one who wants power-setting edge defenders.

**Personnel philosophy:**
- Base ←→ Sub-package heavy — does the DC play base defense (3 LBs or 4 DL)
  on early downs, or does he live in nickel/dime even against the run? The
  modern NFL trend is toward lighter, faster defenses — but a DC who commits
  to nickel as his base needs corners and safeties who can also tackle.
- Safety usage — are safeties deep-field ball hawks, or does the DC bring
  safeties into the box as extra run defenders and blitzers? The "big
  nickel" (3 safeties) and "Star" packages are modern wrinkles.
- Linebacker versatility — does the DC need LBs who can cover (modern,
  pass-defense-oriented) or LBs who fill gaps and tackle (traditional,
  run-defense-oriented)?

**Coverage philosophy:**
- Man ←→ Zone — the fundamental coverage spectrum; man coverage requires
  elite individual cover talent; zone coverage requires discipline,
  communication, and football IQ
- Single-high ←→ Two-high — one deep safety (Cover 1, Cover 3) or two deep
  safeties (Cover 2, Cover 4/Quarters)? This changes everything about how
  the defense defends the run and the deep ball.
- Press ←→ Off — do corners press at the line (disrupts timing but risks
  getting beaten) or play off (safer but gives up easy completions)?
  A press-heavy DC needs corners with elite physical tools and technique.

**Pressure philosophy:**
- Four-man rush ←→ Blitz-heavy — does the DC trust his front four to
  generate pressure, or does he send 5-6 on a regular basis? A blitz-heavy
  DC needs cover corners who can survive 1-on-1, because every blitzer is
  one fewer defender in coverage.
- Disguise — does the DC show his pressure looks pre-snap, or does he
  disguise and rotate at the snap? Disguise creates confusion but requires
  smarter, more disciplined players.
- Zone blitz usage — dropping linemen into coverage while blitzing from
  unexpected positions; a niche but effective tendency that requires
  versatile, high-IQ defenders

**Aggressiveness:**
- Conservative ←→ Aggressive — overall risk tolerance; an aggressive DC
  gambles more (press, blitz, tight coverage) for higher upside but bigger
  breakdowns; a conservative DC bends but doesn't break, limiting big plays
  at the cost of allowing methodical drives
- Situational aggression — some DCs are conservative on early downs but
  aggressive on 3rd down; others dial up pressure when they have a lead but
  play soft from behind

### What this means for roster building

Two coordinators who both get called "zone defense guys" by the media might
want completely different rosters:

```
DC A — "Zone defense coordinator"
  Coverage: Two-high zone (Cover 2 / Cover 4)
  Front: Even, one-gap penetration
  Pressure: Four-man rush, rarely blitzes
  Personnel: Nickel base, athletic LBs who cover

  Needs: Rangy safeties, physical corners who play flats, a dominant
  interior pass rusher (3-technique), coverage LBs

DC B — "Zone defense coordinator"
  Coverage: Single-high zone (Cover 3), heavy disguise
  Front: Odd, two-gap with edge speed
  Pressure: Moderate blitz, zone blitz concepts
  Personnel: Base 3-4 on early downs, nickel on passing downs

  Needs: Space-eating nose tackle, versatile OLBs, an elite free safety,
  smart defenders who can handle assignment complexity
```

Same label. Completely different rosters. This is why the coaching hire IS
the scheme decision — and why "what scheme do we run?" is actually dozens of
smaller questions about tendencies and principles.

## Foundational Concepts

While coaches blend and customize, their tendencies draw from established
football concepts. These are the building blocks — the vocabulary of the
game. A coach's system is a combination of these elements, weighted by his
preferences and adapted to his personnel.

### Offensive foundations

**West Coast offense:**
The Bill Walsh revolution. Short, precise passing replaces the running game
as the ball-control mechanism. Timing routes, yards after catch, methodical
drives. The foundation for much of modern passing. Demands accuracy, route
precision, and receivers who make people miss after the catch.

**Air Raid / spread passing:**
Simplified route concepts, high tempo, spread formations. The Mike Leach
tree and its descendants. Four or five wide receivers, spread the defense
horizontally, attack with speed and space. The college-to-pro pipeline has
brought Air Raid concepts into almost every NFL offense. Demands quick
decision-making from the QB and receivers who win in space.

**Pro-style / multiple:**
The traditional NFL offense. Balanced run/pass, multiple formations, relies
on execution and versatility. Under center, play-action, deep drops, full-
field reads. Requires a well-rounded roster — no single weak link can hide.
The most demanding system for a quarterback because nothing is simplified.

**Zone run scheme:**
Outside zone, inside zone, wide zone — a family of run concepts built on
athletic offensive linemen who reach, climb, and create movement at the
point of attack. The runner reads the blocks and cuts accordingly. The
Shanahan/Kubiak tree popularized this approach. Demands mobile, technically
sound linemen and patient, decisive runners with vision.

**Gap/power run scheme:**
Power, counter, trap, pull — a family of run concepts built on creating
numerical advantages at the point of attack through pulling linemen and
lead blockers. Demands strong, physical linemen and downhill runners who
can press the hole and finish runs.

**RPO integration:**
Run-pass options attached to the run game. The QB reads a defensive player
post-snap and decides to hand off, throw a quick pass, or (in some designs)
pull and throw deeper. A modern staple that most offenses now incorporate to
some degree. Demands a QB with elite processing speed and an OL that can
handle the ambiguity.

**Option concepts:**
Read-option, triple-option, zone-read, and related concepts that use the
QB as a running threat. A continuum from pure option (flexbone, service
academies) to modern zone-read concepts that most mobile QBs now use.
Demands an athletic QB willing to take hits.

**Motion and misdirection:**
Pre-snap motion, jet sweeps, play-action — concepts that manipulate
defenders' eyes and create false keys. Heavy-motion offenses (Shanahan,
McVay, McDaniel) use motion diagnostically (reading how the defense
adjusts) and schematically (creating blocks, pulling defenders out of
position). Adds complexity but creates mismatches.

### Defensive foundations

**Even front (4-down):**
Four down linemen, typically two defensive tackles and two defensive ends.
The classic NFL front. Generates pass rush from the front four, strong
against the run, simple gap assignments. Modern variants include the wide-9
alignment (edges set wide for speed rushing).

**Odd front (3-down):**
Three down linemen with four linebackers. More versatile pre-snap because
the defense can show multiple looks — any of the four LBs might rush, drop,
or blitz. Demands a space-eating nose tackle and hybrid edge/OLB types.

**Nickel / sub-package:**
Extra defensive backs replace linebackers. In the modern NFL, most teams
play nickel (5 DBs) on 60%+ of snaps, making it effectively the base
defense. Some teams go even further with dime (6 DBs) or "big nickel"
(3 safeties). Demands quality DB depth and versatile safeties.

**Cover 2 / Tampa 2:**
Two deep safeties split the field. Corners play the flats. In Tampa 2,
the MLB drops deep into the middle zone, requiring an athletic LB who can
run. Limits big plays but can be vulnerable between the safeties and to
seam routes.

**Cover 3:**
Single high safety, three deep zones (safety and two corners). Strong
against vertical passing, puts an extra defender in the box for run
support. Vulnerable underneath, especially to short/intermediate routes
in the flat and curl zones.

**Cover 4 / Quarters:**
Four defenders split the deep field into quarters. A two-high shell that
is strong against both the run and the pass. The modern "umbrella" defense
that many teams now use as their base coverage. Adaptable but requires
smart, disciplined defenders.

**Man coverage / press-man:**
Individual matchups. Each defender is responsible for a specific receiver.
Press technique at the line disrupts timing but risks getting beaten by
speed or release moves. Demands elite individual talent in the secondary —
you can't scheme around a corner who loses his matchup.

**Zone blitz:**
The Dick LeBeau innovation. Drop defensive linemen into coverage, blitz
linebackers and defensive backs from unexpected positions. Creates
confusion in blocking assignments and quarterback reads. Demands versatile,
high-IQ defenders who can handle unusual responsibilities.

## Player Archetypes

Archetypes are the bridge between raw attributes and scheme fit. An archetype
is a **role within a scheme** defined by the attribute profile it demands.
The same position on the roster might house very different archetypes
depending on what the coach's tendencies require.

A player doesn't have a fixed archetype label. A player has attributes. The
archetype describes what the scheme needs, and the fit — or lack of fit —
emerges from whether the player's attributes match the role's demands.

### Quarterback archetypes

**Pocket passer:**
Lives in the pocket, wins with arm talent, accuracy, anticipation, and
pre-snap reads. The classic NFL QB model.

- **Primary attributes**: Accuracy (short, medium, deep), arm strength,
  football IQ, decision-making, anticipation, composure
- **Secondary attributes**: Touch, release, pocket awareness
- **Physical profile**: Size matters (taller QBs see the field better), speed
  is irrelevant
- **Scheme fit**: Pro-style, West Coast, timing-based passing offenses;
  these QBs thrive in systems with full-field reads and structured
  progressions
- **Example archetype**: The 6'4" QB who can't run but carves up defenses
  from the pocket with pinpoint accuracy and preternatural anticipation

**Dual-threat QB:**
Dangerous with both his arm and his legs. Can extend plays, pick up first
downs on scrambles, and force defenses to account for the run.

- **Primary attributes**: Speed, acceleration, agility, accuracy (on the run),
  decision-making, football IQ
- **Secondary attributes**: Arm strength, elusiveness, composure
- **Physical profile**: Athletic, mobile, may sacrifice size for speed
- **Scheme fit**: RPO-heavy, option concepts, zone-read, pistol; systems
  that use the QB as a running threat and incorporate designed QB runs
- **Risk factor**: Dual-threat QBs take more hits; durability is a hidden
  concern that can end careers early

**Game manager:**
Doesn't make the spectacular throw, but doesn't lose games either. Gets the
ball to playmakers, protects possession, and executes the scheme reliably.

- **Primary attributes**: Decision-making, composure, consistency, accuracy
  (short), football IQ
- **Secondary attributes**: Touch, leadership
- **Physical profile**: Doesn't matter — this archetype is defined by
  mental attributes, not physical ones
- **Scheme fit**: Power run offenses, strong-defense teams, any system where
  the QB isn't asked to carry the offense; the game manager's value is
  inversely proportional to how much the offense depends on QB play
- **The undervalued archetype**: In a league that chases the next franchise
  QB, a game manager on a cheap contract with an elite defense and running
  game might be the smartest roster construction in the league

**Gunslinger:**
High-risk, high-reward arm talent. Launches bombs, throws into tight
windows, makes jaw-dropping plays — and backbreaking turnovers.

- **Primary attributes**: Arm strength, accuracy (deep), composure (or lack
  thereof — a gunslinger with low composure is a disaster in big moments)
- **Secondary attributes**: Touch (often lacking), decision-making (the key
  variable — a gunslinger with elite decision-making is a franchise QB; one
  with poor decision-making is a turnover machine)
- **Scheme fit**: Vertical passing offenses, Air Raid, any system that pushes
  the ball downfield; badly fits conservative, ball-control systems
- **The coaching challenge**: Can you coach the gunslinger to throw it away?
  Can you give him a system that channels his aggression productively?
  This archetype is the ultimate coaching test.

### Running back archetypes

**Power / downhill back:**
Between-the-tackles runner who punishes defenders. Falls forward, breaks
tackles, wears defenses down in the 4th quarter.

- **Primary attributes**: Strength, ball carrying, durability, stamina
- **Secondary attributes**: Speed (enough to hit the hole, not necessarily
  breakaway), acceleration
- **Physical profile**: 220-240 lbs, thick build, low center of gravity
- **Scheme fit**: Gap/power run schemes, short-yardage/goal-line packages,
  cold-weather and late-season football

**Scat back / receiving back:**
A weapon in the passing game. Lines up in the slot, runs routes, catches
the ball in space, creates mismatches against linebackers.

- **Primary attributes**: Catching, route running, elusiveness, run after
  catch, agility
- **Secondary attributes**: Speed, acceleration
- **Physical profile**: 185-205 lbs, quick, fluid
- **Scheme fit**: West Coast, spread, pass-heavy offenses, any system that
  uses the RB as a receiver; often a change-of-pace/third-down specialist

**Zone runner:**
Patient, one-cut runner with elite vision. Reads blocks, sets up defenders,
and hits the crease hard.

- **Primary attributes**: Football IQ (vision), acceleration, agility, ball
  carrying
- **Secondary attributes**: Speed, elusiveness
- **Physical profile**: Varies — the key is processing speed and lateral
  quickness, not a specific body type
- **Scheme fit**: Zone run schemes (inside zone, outside zone, wide zone);
  a zone runner in a gap scheme may underperform because his patience reads
  as indecisiveness

**All-purpose back:**
Does everything well, nothing at an elite level. Runs, catches, blocks,
stays on the field for all three downs.

- **Primary attributes**: Balanced profile — ball carrying, catching, pass
  blocking all need to be solid
- **Secondary attributes**: Durability (three-down backs take a beating),
  stamina
- **Scheme fit**: Pro-style, any system that doesn't substitute heavily;
  the all-purpose back's value is that you don't have to tip your play
  call with personnel changes

### Wide receiver archetypes

**X receiver (flanker / boundary receiver):**
The alpha. Lines up on the outside, wins contested catches, high-points the
football, and dominates one-on-one matchups. He's the "go get it" receiver
you throw to in single coverage.

- **Primary attributes**: Contested catching, catching, route running, speed
- **Secondary attributes**: Jumping, strength, size
- **Physical profile**: 6'1"+ and 200+ lbs; size and length matter here
  because this receiver plays above the rim
- **Scheme fit**: Any offense needs an X, but vertical passing systems and
  offenses that isolate receivers benefit most; a coach who runs heavy
  play-action with deep shots needs an elite X

**Z receiver (split end / field receiver):**
The versatile outside receiver. May line up on either side, often in motion.
Wins with speed, route running, and the ability to threaten at every level
of the field.

- **Primary attributes**: Route running, speed, acceleration, catching
- **Secondary attributes**: Run after catch, elusiveness
- **Physical profile**: Varies — the Z is defined by skill, not body type
- **Scheme fit**: Motion-heavy offenses (Shanahan tree), systems that use
  pre-snap movement diagnostically, offenses that need speed to stretch
  the field horizontally

**Slot receiver:**
Works the middle of the field. Quick releases off the line, sharp route
running, catches in traffic, creates yards after the catch in space.

- **Primary attributes**: Route running, catching, run after catch, agility,
  acceleration
- **Secondary attributes**: Composure (catching over the middle with a safety
  coming), toughness
- **Physical profile**: 5'9"-6'0", 180-200 lbs typically; quickness over
  size
- **Scheme fit**: West Coast, spread, any offense that attacks the short/
  intermediate middle of the field; some offenses live in 11 personnel and
  the slot is the most important receiver on the team

**Deep threat / speedster:**
One thing: run fast. Stretches the defense vertically, opens up underneath
routes for everyone else, and occasionally catches a bomb for a touchdown.

- **Primary attributes**: Speed, acceleration, catching (particularly deep
  ball tracking)
- **Secondary attributes**: Route running (limited tree — mostly go routes,
  posts, and deep crossers)
- **Physical profile**: Fast. That's it. Can be small if he's fast enough.
- **Scheme fit**: Vertical passing offenses, offenses that need to create
  space underneath; even a conservative offense benefits from a deep threat
  who forces the safety to respect the deep ball
- **The one-trick question**: A pure deep threat who can't run other routes
  becomes predictable. Defenses bracket him and take away the deep ball. His
  value depends on whether the rest of the offense can punish the defense
  for devoting resources to stopping him.

### Tight end archetypes

**Blocking tight end / Y tight end:**
An extra offensive lineman who happens to line up at tight end. Sets the
edge in the run game, chips pass rushers, and occasionally leaks out for a
play-action target.

- **Primary attributes**: Run blocking, pass blocking, strength
- **Secondary attributes**: Catching (enough to be a credible decoy)
- **Physical profile**: 6'4"+, 255+ lbs, built like a lineman
- **Scheme fit**: Power run, pro-style, any offense that values a physical
  run game at the point of attack; essential in 12 and 13 personnel
  groupings

**Receiving tight end / move tight end:**
A matchup nightmare. Lines up inline, in the slot, split out wide — wherever
he can exploit a coverage mismatch. Too big for corners, too fast for
linebackers.

- **Primary attributes**: Route running, catching, run after catch, speed,
  agility
- **Secondary attributes**: Contested catching, football IQ
- **Physical profile**: 6'3"-6'6", 230-250 lbs; athletic enough to win as a
  receiver, big enough that you can't cover him with a DB
- **Scheme fit**: Modern passing offenses, any system that creates mismatches
  in the seam and over the middle; the receiving TE is the most
  scheme-transcendent offensive weapon — he warps coverage by existing

**H-back / fullback-TE hybrid:**
Lines up everywhere — inline, in the backfield, in the slot, in motion.
Does a little of everything: lead blocks, catches checkdowns, chips
defenders, runs option routes. A Swiss Army knife.

- **Primary attributes**: Run blocking, catching, football IQ (must know
  multiple alignment responsibilities), agility
- **Secondary attributes**: Pass blocking, ball carrying
- **Physical profile**: 6'1"-6'4", 240-255 lbs; not the biggest, not the
  fastest, but smart and versatile
- **Scheme fit**: Shanahan-tree offenses, motion-heavy systems, offenses
  that use pre-snap alignment versatility to create diagnostic reads;
  the H-back's value is that the defense doesn't know what he's doing
  on any given play

**Fullback / lead blocker:**
The dinosaur that refuses to go extinct. A pure lead blocker who lines up
in the backfield and clears a path for the running back. In a league that
has nearly abandoned the position, carrying a fullback is a philosophical
statement about how you want to play football.

- **Primary attributes**: Run blocking, strength, durability
- **Secondary attributes**: Ball carrying (short-yardage dive plays),
  catching (enough to be a play-action threat out of the flat)
- **Physical profile**: 5'11"-6'2", 240-260 lbs; built like a bowling ball
- **Scheme fit**: Power run, gap schemes, goal-line packages, any offense
  that uses 21 or 22 personnel groupings; the fullback is nearly useless
  in spread or pass-heavy schemes, which is why most teams don't carry one
- **The roster spot question**: This is the real debate. Carrying a fullback
  means *not* carrying a 6th WR, a 4th safety, or an extra pass rusher.
  If your fullback plays 10 offensive snaps per game, is that roster spot
  worth it? In a power run offense, absolutely — he's the lead blocker on
  your most important plays. In a spread offense, he's a wasted roster
  spot. The fullback is the purest example of how scheme dictates roster
  construction.

### Offensive line archetypes

**Mauler / road grader:**
Physically dominant run blocker. Drives defenders off the ball, creates
movement at the point of attack, finishes blocks in the dirt.

- **Primary attributes**: Strength, run blocking, durability
- **Secondary attributes**: Stamina, size
- **Physical profile**: 6'4"+, 315+ lbs, massive
- **Scheme fit**: Gap/power run schemes, ground-and-pound offenses, any
  system that wins by physically imposing its will at the line of scrimmage
- **Trade-off**: Maulers often struggle in pass protection because their
  anchor relies on aggression and forward lean — which pass rushers can
  exploit with counter moves

**Technician / pass protector:**
Wins with footwork, hand placement, and leverage rather than raw power.
Mirrors speed rushers, anchors against bull rushes with technique, and
rarely gets beaten because his fundamentals are sound.

- **Primary attributes**: Pass blocking, agility, football IQ
- **Secondary attributes**: Strength (enough to anchor), run blocking
- **Physical profile**: Varies — the technician is defined by technique, not
  body type
- **Scheme fit**: Pass-heavy offenses, spread, Air Raid; any system that
  drops back 40+ times per game needs technicians who can sustain
  protection

**Athletic / zone blocker:**
Moves well laterally, reaches to the second level, climbs to linebackers,
and combo blocks effectively. The modern NFL lineman.

- **Primary attributes**: Agility, acceleration, run blocking, football IQ
- **Secondary attributes**: Pass blocking, strength
- **Physical profile**: 6'3"-6'6", 300-320 lbs; lighter and more athletic
  than a mauler
- **Scheme fit**: Zone run schemes (inside zone, outside zone, wide zone);
  this archetype is increasingly dominant as zone concepts have taken over
  the NFL; a zone blocker in a gap/power scheme may underperform because
  his strength doesn't match the assignment demands

**Versatile / swing lineman:**
Can play multiple positions along the line. The ultimate roster flexibility
piece — he might be your starting right guard and your backup left tackle.

- **Primary attributes**: Football IQ (must know multiple positions'
  assignments), pass blocking, run blocking — all need to be solid, none
  need to be elite
- **Secondary attributes**: Agility, strength
- **Scheme fit**: Any system values versatility, but it's particularly
  important for teams with roster constraints or injury concerns; a
  versatile lineman might be your 6th OL who dresses on game day and
  can step in at any of the five positions

### Defensive line archetypes

**Edge rusher / speed rusher:**
Gets to the quarterback with speed, bend, and pass rush moves. The most
valuable non-QB archetype in football because sacks change games.

- **Primary attributes**: Speed, acceleration, pass rushing, agility
- **Secondary attributes**: Strength (enough to hold the edge), stamina
- **Physical profile**: 6'2"-6'5", 240-265 lbs; built for speed off the edge
- **Scheme fit**: Any defense that generates pressure from the edge; speed
  rushers are particularly valuable in four-man rush systems where the DC
  trusts his front to create pressure without blitzing

**Power edge / edge setter:**
Wins with strength and power rather than speed. Sets the edge against the
run, bull-rushes offensive tackles, and collapses the pocket. Less flashy
than the speed rusher, but more complete.

- **Primary attributes**: Strength, run defense, pass rushing, block shedding
- **Secondary attributes**: Durability, stamina
- **Physical profile**: 6'3"-6'6", 265-285 lbs; bigger and more physical
  than the speed rusher
- **Scheme fit**: Two-gap defenses, run-first defensive fronts, teams that
  prioritize stopping the run; also valuable as the complementary edge
  opposite a speed rusher

**3-technique / penetrating DT:**
The interior disruptor. Lined up between the guard and tackle, he shoots
gaps, collapses the pocket from the inside, and blows up run plays in the
backfield. Aaron Donald is the archetype.

- **Primary attributes**: Acceleration, pass rushing, agility, strength
- **Secondary attributes**: Block shedding, speed (for a DT)
- **Physical profile**: 6'1"-6'4", 280-305 lbs; undersized for a DT but
  explosively quick
- **Scheme fit**: One-gap, penetration-based defenses; the 3-technique is
  the engine of an aggressive defensive line; less valuable in two-gap
  systems that ask DTs to hold ground rather than penetrate

**Nose tackle / space eater:**
Occupies two gaps, absorbs double teams, and clogs the middle of the field.
Won't show up on the stat sheet. Will show up on the film as the reason
your linebackers are running free to make tackles.

- **Primary attributes**: Strength, run defense, durability, size
- **Secondary attributes**: Block shedding, stamina
- **Physical profile**: 6'0"-6'4", 320-350 lbs; massive, immovable
- **Scheme fit**: Two-gap defenses, odd fronts (3-4), run-stuffing defensive
  philosophies; the nose tackle is the most scheme-dependent DL archetype —
  a great nose tackle in a penetrating defense is wasted because his
  strength (occupying space) isn't what the scheme asks for

### Linebacker archetypes

**Off-ball run thumper:**
Fills gaps, takes on blocks, and makes tackles between the tackles. The
traditional middle linebacker who stops the run and doesn't leave the box.

- **Primary attributes**: Tackling, run defense, block shedding, strength
- **Secondary attributes**: Football IQ, composure
- **Physical profile**: 6'1"-6'3", 240-255 lbs; big, physical, downhill
- **Scheme fit**: Run-first defenses, traditional 4-3 and 3-4 schemes; this
  archetype is increasingly rare in a passing league because he can't stay
  on the field in nickel — if he can't cover, he's a two-down player

**Coverage linebacker:**
Runs with tight ends, drops into zones, plays the pass as well as the run.
The modern NFL linebacker who can stay on the field in nickel packages
because he doesn't create a coverage liability.

- **Primary attributes**: Zone coverage, man coverage, speed, agility,
  football IQ
- **Secondary attributes**: Tackling, run defense
- **Physical profile**: 6'0"-6'3", 225-245 lbs; lighter, faster, more fluid
- **Scheme fit**: Modern defenses, nickel-heavy systems, Cover 2/Tampa 2
  (which demands an athletic MLB), any defense that faces passing offenses
  regularly (which in the modern NFL is every defense)
- **The replacement**: In many modern defenses, this archetype has replaced
  the run thumper entirely — the game has evolved to demand LBs who can
  cover, and run-stopping-only LBs have been schemed into obsolescence

**Edge / OLB hybrid:**
Stands up on the edge in odd fronts, rushes the passer, drops into coverage,
and sets the edge against the run. The 3-4 OLB who does everything.

- **Primary attributes**: Pass rushing, speed, run defense, man coverage
  (enough to handle a TE)
- **Secondary attributes**: Block shedding, agility
- **Physical profile**: 6'2"-6'5", 240-260 lbs; a tweener between DE and LB
  who fits neither traditional position cleanly
- **Scheme fit**: 3-4 defenses, odd fronts, multiple-look defenses that
  disguise their rush; this archetype's value is versatility — he can rush
  and drop, and the offense doesn't know which one pre-snap
- **The evaluation challenge**: This is the hardest archetype to scout because
  the player must be excellent at two very different skills. A pass-rushing
  specialist disguised as a hybrid is a common draft mistake.

### Secondary archetypes

**Boundary corner:**
Lines up on the outside, typically on the defense's right side (facing the
offense's best receiver). Plays press or off coverage, tracks a single
receiver, and is responsible for his man or his deep third depending on the
call.

- **Primary attributes**: Man coverage, speed, agility, composure
- **Secondary attributes**: Jumping, anticipation
- **Physical profile**: 5'11"-6'2", 185-200 lbs; length and speed matter;
  long arms and fluid hips are the physical hallmarks
- **Scheme fit**: Man-heavy defenses need elite boundary corners because
  there's nowhere to hide; zone defenses can mask a weaker boundary corner
  with safety help

**Slot corner / nickel back:**
Covers the slot receiver. A different skill set than the boundary corner —
the slot defender works in traffic, covers quick-breaking routes, tackles
in space, and often faces the opponent's most elusive receiver.

- **Primary attributes**: Zone coverage, agility, tackling, anticipation
- **Secondary attributes**: Man coverage, acceleration
- **Physical profile**: 5'9"-6'0", 185-200 lbs; quicker and more compact
  than a boundary corner; some teams use undersized safeties here
- **Scheme fit**: Any defense that plays nickel (which is every defense in
  the modern game); the slot corner is a starter, not a sub-package luxury;
  a weak slot corner is exploitable on 60%+ of defensive snaps
- **The hidden value**: Slot corner might be the most undervalued archetype
  in the game because fans and media focus on the outside corners. A GM who
  invests in elite slot coverage has a structural advantage.

**Free safety / centerfielder:**
Plays deep, covers ground sideline to sideline, reads the quarterback's
eyes, and makes plays on the ball. The last line of defense.

- **Primary attributes**: Zone coverage, speed, anticipation, football IQ,
  catching
- **Secondary attributes**: Jumping, composure
- **Physical profile**: 5'11"-6'2", 195-210 lbs; range and ball skills
  matter more than size
- **Scheme fit**: Single-high defenses (Cover 1, Cover 3) that ask the free
  safety to patrol the deep middle; an elite free safety makes the entire
  defense better because he erases mistakes underneath

**Box safety / strong safety:**
Plays closer to the line of scrimmage. Run support, blitz weapon, covers
tight ends, tackles in the open field. A hybrid between safety and
linebacker.

- **Primary attributes**: Tackling, run defense, man coverage, strength
- **Secondary attributes**: Zone coverage, speed, blitz ability
- **Physical profile**: 6'0"-6'3", 210-225 lbs; bigger and more physical
  than a free safety
- **Scheme fit**: Defenses that need a run defender in the secondary, 8-man
  box looks, teams that blitz from the safety position; the box safety's
  coverage limitations can be exposed in two-high schemes that ask him to
  play deep
- **The modern tension**: The NFL increasingly wants safeties who can play
  both deep and in the box. The pure box safety is being squeezed by the
  versatile "do-everything" safety archetype — but a box safety with elite
  run support and blitz ability still has value in the right scheme.

**Versatile safety / positionless DB:**
The modern ideal. Can play deep, in the box, in the slot, on the blitz —
wherever the defense needs him on any given play. The do-everything
defensive back.

- **Primary attributes**: Zone coverage, man coverage, tackling, football IQ,
  speed
- **Secondary attributes**: Run defense, anticipation, composure
- **Physical profile**: 6'0"-6'2", 200-215 lbs; athletic enough to cover,
  physical enough to tackle, smart enough to play multiple positions
- **Scheme fit**: Any defense, but particularly valuable in disguise-heavy
  systems where pre-snap alignment is designed to confuse the offense; the
  versatile safety lets the DC show one look and play another

### Special teams archetypes

**Power kicker:**
Big leg. Makes 55-yarders. Might miss the 38-yarder because accuracy isn't
his thing.

- **Primary attributes**: Kicking power
- **Secondary attributes**: Kicking accuracy, composure
- **Scheme fit**: Teams that play in domes or warm weather, offenses that
  stall in the red zone (more long FG attempts), aggressive coaches who
  want the option to kick from 55+

**Precision kicker:**
Won't miss inside 45. Won't attempt from 55. Reliable, consistent, boring
in the best possible way.

- **Primary attributes**: Kicking accuracy, composure, consistency
- **Secondary attributes**: Kicking power
- **Scheme fit**: Any team, but particularly valuable for conservative
  coaches and playoff-caliber teams where a missed kick is the difference
  between a Super Bowl and a "what if"

**Punter — directional / weapon:**
Pins opponents inside the 10. Varies hang time, direction, and distance
to control field position. Doesn't just kick it far — kicks it smart.

- **Primary attributes**: Punting accuracy, punting power, football IQ
- **Secondary attributes**: Composure (coffin corner punts under pressure)
- **Scheme fit**: Teams with elite defenses that win the field position
  battle; a great punter on a great defense is a cheat code because every
  opponent drive starts with a long field

**Long snapper:**
You don't notice him when he's good. You absolutely notice him when he's
bad. A botched snap loses games.

- **Primary attributes**: Snap accuracy, consistency, composure
- **Scheme fit**: Every team. The long snapper is the ultimate "you don't
  know what you have until it's gone" position.

**Return specialist:**
Electric with the ball in his hands in the open field. Turns a routine
kickoff into a 50-yard return, turns a punt into a house call. The return
specialist might be a WR or DB who doubles in the role, or he might be a
roster spot dedicated purely to the return game.

- **Primary attributes**: Speed, acceleration, elusiveness, agility, ball
  carrying
- **Secondary attributes**: Composure (fielding a punt with a gunner in
  your face), football IQ (knowing when to fair catch, when to let it go,
  when to take it out of the end zone)
- **Physical profile**: Fast and elusive; size is irrelevant — what matters
  is burst, vision, and the ability to make the first man miss
- **Scheme fit**: Every team benefits from a dangerous returner, but the
  question is whether you dedicate a roster spot to it or use a player
  who contributes elsewhere. A return specialist who can't play offense or
  defense is a luxury roster spot — worth it if he's elite, a waste if he's
  merely good. Some STCs design their return schemes around the returner's
  skill set (speed returner gets schemes that set up the sideline; elusive
  returner gets schemes that funnel to the middle and let him make people
  miss).
- **The hidden weapon**: A great return specialist changes field position
  math for the entire game. If your opponent knows a touchback gives you
  the ball at the 25, but a kick gives the returner a chance to flip the
  field, they're making different decisions. That ripple effect is real
  and undervalued.

**Gunner:**
The kamikaze missile on punt coverage. Sprints down the field, avoids or
defeats two blockers, and makes the tackle on the returner. The gunner is
pure special teams — his value is entirely in his ability to cover kicks.

- **Primary attributes**: Speed, tackling, acceleration, agility, durability
- **Secondary attributes**: Strength (defeating jams at the line), composure
  (making tackles in space at full speed)
- **Physical profile**: 5'10"-6'2", 190-210 lbs; typically a WR or DB body
  type with exceptional speed and fearlessness
- **Scheme fit**: Every punt team needs gunners, but the question is whether
  you carry a player whose primary value is gunner duty. Elite gunners are
  often WRs or DBs who can contribute on offense or defense — Matthew Slater
  is the archetype of a player who carved out a Hall of Fame career almost
  entirely on special teams. A great STC maximizes gunner talent; a bad STC
  wastes it.
- **The roster puzzle**: The gunner epitomizes the 53-man roster game. Do
  you keep the 5th corner who's an elite gunner, or the 5th corner who's
  a better defensive player but a liability on coverage teams? That
  decision is the difference between a punt return touchdown against you
  and a punt downed at the 8-yard line. Special teams coaches live and die
  by these roster fringe decisions.

**Kick/punt coverage teams (the unsung units):**
Beyond the gunner, the entire coverage unit matters. Coverage teams are
built from players across the roster — linebackers, safeties, running backs,
tight ends — who double as special teamers. A player's special teams value
is a hidden factor in roster construction:

- A backup linebacker who's an elite coverage player might make the roster
  over a more talented LB who's a liability on special teams
- STCs identify and develop special teams contributors from across the roster
- Teams that neglect special teams personnel give up return touchdowns and
  lose field position — which loses close games
- The "core four" special teamers (players who play on multiple ST units)
  are among the most valuable depth players on the roster, even if they
  never start on offense or defense

## Scheme Fit

Scheme fit is not a separate rating. It's **emergent from the interaction
between a player's attributes and the demands of the coach's tendencies.**

There is no hidden "scheme fit: 72" on any player. Instead, the coach's
tendencies define which attributes matter most, and the player either has
those attributes or he doesn't. The degree of alignment IS the fit.

### How it works

A West Coast OC's system weights short accuracy, touch, decision-making,
and composure heavily. A QB with 85 short accuracy, 80 touch, and 75
decision-making will perform well in this system. The same QB in an Air
Raid system — which weights quick release, deep accuracy, and arm strength
more heavily — might perform worse because the system is asking for
attributes he doesn't have at the same level.

The inverse is equally true. A cannon-armed gunslinger with 90 arm strength
and 60 short accuracy will be exposed in a West Coast system that asks him
to make 47 short timing throws per game. Put him in a vertical passing
offense that asks him to throw 8 deep shots per game, and his arm strength
becomes the defining weapon.

**Nothing about the player changed. The context changed.**

### The layers of fit

Scheme fit operates at multiple levels:

1. **Tendency fit** — does the player's attribute profile align with what the
   coach's tendencies demand? A speed rusher fits a four-man-rush DC's
   scheme. A run-stuffing nose tackle fits a two-gap DC's scheme.

2. **Archetype fit** — does the player match the archetype the scheme needs
   at his position? The coach needs a zone runner; your back is a power
   runner. He'll underperform because the scheme is asking him to read
   and cut, but his skill is in pressing the hole and running through
   tackles.

3. **Complementary fit** — does the player complement the other players in
   the scheme? An elite X receiver paired with a deep-threat Z and a
   reliable slot gives the offense three distinct weapons. Three players
   who all run the same routes create redundancy that the defense can
   exploit.

4. **System familiarity** — has the player been in this type of system
   before? A veteran corner who's played man coverage his entire career
   will adapt to a new man-heavy scheme faster than one who's been in
   zone his whole career. This is a temporal factor — familiarity improves
   over time as the player learns the system.

### Scheme fit and player value

This is where scheme fit intersects with the market and creates
inefficiencies:

- A player who's a **poor fit for his current team** might be underperforming
  his true ability. If you can acquire him and put him in a better scheme
  fit, you've found a market inefficiency. This is the trade acquisition
  that looks confusing on paper but makes perfect sense once you see him
  in your system.
- A player who's a **great fit for his current team** might be overperforming
  relative to his raw attributes. If he hits free agency and joins a
  team with a different scheme, he might regress. This is the free agency
  signing that looks great on paper and disappoints immediately.
- A draft prospect's scheme fit depends on **the coach you haven't hired
  yet** — or the coach you might fire next year. Drafting a 3-4 nose tackle
  when your DC runs a 3-4 is smart. It's less smart if you fire that DC
  next offseason and hire a 4-3 guy.

### What the user sees

You never see a scheme fit number. What you see:

- **Performance** — a player performing below expectations might be a scheme
  fit issue, a coaching issue, or a talent issue. You diagnose it the same
  way a real GM does: by watching, studying, and consulting your staff.
- **Coach's depth chart** — if your coach is benching a player you think is
  talented, maybe the coach sees a scheme fit problem you don't.
- **Stats in context** — a running back averaging 3.2 YPC in a zone scheme
  might average 4.5 in a gap scheme. The stats alone don't tell you why.
  Your job is to figure it out.
- **Scout reports** — when evaluating a trade target or free agent, your
  scouts assess scheme fit based on their understanding of your coach's
  system. But scouts can be wrong about scheme fit — especially if the
  scout doesn't fully understand the coach's tendencies.

## How Schemes Interact with Other Systems

### Scouting and Drafting

Your coach's tendencies determine your draft board. Two GMs can look at the
same edge rusher and see completely different values:

```
Edge Rusher Prospect — "Jordan Ellis"
  Speed: 92  Strength: 71  Pass Rush Moves: 85  Run Defense: 65

  DC with speed-rush, four-man-rush tendencies:
    ELITE prospect — his speed and pass rush ability are exactly what
    this defense needs off the edge

  DC with two-gap, run-first tendencies:
    POOR fit — not strong enough to set the edge in a system that
    asks the DE to control the C-gap against the run
```

This means positional scarcity is scheme-dependent. A "weak" draft class for
one team might be deep for another.

### Free Agency

Players on the market have scheme histories. A corner who thrived in a Cover 3
system might struggle transitioning to man-heavy press coverage. This is a
factor in both the player's decision and your evaluation.

### Coaching Staff

Coaches bring their tendencies with them. Hiring decisions cascade:

- You hire an OC from the Air Raid tree
- Your roster was built for power run — big linemen, a bruiser back, a
  blocking tight end
- Now you're facing a multi-year transition: draft speed players, release
  power players, or accept a mismatch and underperform while you retool
- Meanwhile, your existing players' development is disrupted — a power-
  blocking guard isn't suddenly going to become a zone-scheme athlete

See [Coaches](./coaches.md) for the full coaching system.

### Game Simulation

Tendency vs. tendency matchups influence outcomes. This isn't rock-paper-
scissors — talent still matters most — but schematic edges are real:

- A spread offense with four WRs against a base defense that won't go to
  nickel creates mismatches
- A power run team in bad weather against a finesse defense has an edge
- A zone blitz can rattle a young QB but a veteran reads it and audibles
- Halftime adjustments from good coordinators can swing a game
- A coach whose tendencies have become predictable gets exploited by
  opponents who study the tape; this is the "figured out by week 8"
  phenomenon

### Scheme Transitions

Changing coaches means changing tendencies, and changing tendencies is costly:

- Players lose their scheme fit alignment during transition (1-2 season
  adjustment)
- Some players may never fit the new tendencies and need to be moved
- Young players adapt faster than veterans
- Coaching staff continuity accelerates the transition
- The payoff is worth it if the new coaching staff better fits your long-term
  roster vision

This is why firing a coach is never just about the coach — it's about the
cascade. A new DC who runs a Cover 3 inherits a roster built for Cover 2.
Your $15M free safety you signed for his range is now a box safety in a
scheme that needs a different skill set. The coaching hire decision IS the
scheme decision, and both are roster decisions.

This creates one of the game's deepest strategic questions: do you hire
coaches who fit your current players, or draft players who fit the coaches
you want?
