# Schemes & Strategy

Football schemes are the connective tissue of the entire game. Your team's
scheme defines what kind of players you need, which prospects you value, which
free agents you target, and how your team performs on the field. But you don't
pick a scheme from a menu — **your coaches determine your scheme**. You want to
run a 3-4 defense? Hire a DC who runs a 3-4. You want a spread offense? Hire an
OC who installs one. Your scheme decision is a coaching hire decision, and
changing your scheme means changing your coaches — which ripples through every
other system.

## Why Schemes Matter

In a game without schemes, team-building is simple: get the highest-rated
players at every position. Schemes make it a puzzle. A 90-overall player who
doesn't fit your scheme might perform worse than a 78-overall player who fits
perfectly. This means:

- Two GMs looking at the same draft class build completely different boards
- A player's trade value depends on who's buying
- Coaching hires have multi-year roster implications
- There's no single "correct" way to build a team

## Offensive Schemes

Each offensive scheme has a distinct identity, personnel requirements, and
strengths/weaknesses.

### Pro Style

The traditional NFL offense. Balanced run/pass, multiple formations, relies on
execution and versatility. Requires a well-rounded roster — no single weak link
can hide.

- **Key personnel**: Pocket QB with good pre-snap reads, versatile RB who can
  pass-protect, complete TE who blocks and catches
- **Strengths**: Hard to game-plan against, no obvious weaknesses, adaptable
- **Weaknesses**: Doesn't create mismatches, relies on talent across the board,
  no scheme-driven "free" yards

### Spread

Four or five wide receivers, spread the defense horizontally, attack with speed
and space. Creates mismatches in the passing game.

- **Key personnel**: Accurate QB with quick release, fast WRs who win in space,
  pass-protecting OL
- **Strengths**: Creates favorable matchups, exploits slow LBs in coverage,
  high-variance (big-play potential)
- **Weaknesses**: Vulnerable to pressure, can struggle in short-yardage/goal
  line, pass-heavy means more turnovers

### Run 'n' Gun / Air Raid

Extreme passing volume. Simplified route concepts, high tempo, get the ball out
fast. The Mike Leach tree.

- **Key personnel**: Quick-decision QB, high-volume WRs, OL that can sustain
  pass protection
- **Strengths**: Tempo creates chaos, difficult to prepare for, maximizes
  passing talent
- **Weaknesses**: One-dimensional, susceptible to takeaways, can stall against
  elite secondaries

### West Coast

Short, precise passing replaces the running game as the ball-control mechanism.
Timing routes, yards after catch, methodical drives.

- **Key personnel**: Accurate short-range QB, WRs with great route-running
  and YAC ability, receiving backs
- **Strengths**: Controls clock through passing, low-turnover, hard to defend
  when executed well
- **Weaknesses**: Fewer big plays, requires precision from multiple positions,
  struggles against press coverage

### Option / Flexbone

Read-option, triple-option, and misdirection. Relies on the QB as a runner and
disciplined blocking assignments.

- **Key personnel**: Athletic/mobile QB, tough RBs, physical OL, scheme over
  talent
- **Strengths**: Doesn't need elite talent to compete, controls clock, hard to
  prepare for (uncommon in pro game)
- **Weaknesses**: Injury risk to QB, limits passing game development, hard to
  come from behind

### Power Run / Ground and Pound

Run the ball, control the clock, play physical football. Win the line of
scrimmage.

- **Key personnel**: Big, powerful OL, workhorse RB, TE who can block, QB who
  manages the game
- **Strengths**: Controls time of possession, wears down defenses, lower
  turnover rate, dominates in bad weather
- **Weaknesses**: Predictable, hard to come from behind, requires sustained
  investment in OL

### Pistol

Hybrid of shotgun passing concepts with a downhill run game. Versatile
formation that doesn't tip run/pass.

- **Key personnel**: Dual-threat QB, versatile RB, balanced OL
- **Strengths**: Difficult to read pre-snap, balanced attack, adaptable
- **Weaknesses**: Jack-of-all-trades can be master of none, requires a rare
  QB skill set

### RPO-Heavy

Run-pass options on nearly every play. The QB reads the defense post-snap and
decides to hand off or throw based on defensive alignment.

- **Key personnel**: High football IQ QB who processes fast, athletic OL who
  can block for both run and pass on the same play, WRs who win quickly
- **Strengths**: Puts maximum stress on the defense, exploits overly aggressive
  defenders, efficient
- **Weaknesses**: Penalties (OL downfield on pass), requires elite QB
  processing, can break down against disciplined defenses

## Defensive Schemes

### 4-3

Four down linemen, three linebackers. The classic defense.

- **Key personnel**: Penetrating DT (3-technique), edge-rushing DE, rangy
  weakside LB, athletic MLB
- **Strengths**: Strong against the run, generates pass rush from the front
  four, simple assignments
- **Weaknesses**: Only three LBs to cover, vulnerable to crossing routes over
  the middle

### 3-4

Three down linemen, four linebackers. Versatile, disguise-heavy.

- **Key personnel**: Space-eating nose tackle, versatile OLBs who rush and
  drop, athletic ILBs
- **Strengths**: Versatile blitz packages, hard to identify the pass rusher
  pre-snap, extra LB helps in coverage
- **Weaknesses**: Requires a rare nose tackle archetype, OLBs must be true
  hybrid athletes

### Nickel / Dime (Sub-packages)

Extra defensive backs replace linebackers. The modern passing-down defense that
many teams now use as a base.

- **Key personnel**: Quality CB depth (need 3+ solid corners), slot corner
  specialist, coverage-capable safety
- **Strengths**: Matches up with spread offenses, better in coverage, forces
  offenses out of heavy sets
- **Weaknesses**: Vulnerable to the run, smaller defenders get bullied at the
  point of attack

### Cover 2 / Tampa 2

Two deep safeties split the field, cornerbacks play flats, MLB drops deep into
the middle zone.

- **Key personnel**: Athletic MLB who can run with TEs, physical press corners,
  rangy safeties
- **Strengths**: Limits big plays, good against outside runs, takes away the
  deep ball
- **Weaknesses**: Vulnerable to the deep middle (between the safeties), seam
  routes, relies heavily on MLB range

### Cover 3

Single high safety, three deep zones. Strong against vertical passing.

- **Key personnel**: Elite free safety (center field), physical corners who
  can press and play zone, run-stopping strong safety
- **Strengths**: Sound against deep passes, extra defender in the box, good
  run support
- **Weaknesses**: Vulnerable to short/intermediate routes underneath, flat
  areas can be exploited

### Man-Heavy / Press

Aggressive man-to-man coverage with press technique at the line. Relies on
individual talent in coverage.

- **Key personnel**: Elite corners who can win 1-on-1, versatile safety,
  pass rushers who can get home quickly (coverage can't hold forever)
- **Strengths**: Eliminates windows, disrupts timing, frees up defenders to
  blitz
- **Weaknesses**: Beaten by elite route runners, pick plays, vulnerable to
  big plays if beaten

### Zone Blitz

Drops linemen into coverage while blitzing from unexpected positions. Designed
to confuse blocking assignments and QBs.

- **Key personnel**: Versatile DL who can drop into zones, blitzing LBs and
  DBs, smart defensive players who can handle unusual assignments
- **Strengths**: Creates confusion, generates pressure from unexpected angles,
  disguises coverage
- **Weaknesses**: Relies on deception (less effective as opponents adjust),
  complex to execute, mistakes lead to big plays

## How Schemes Interact with Other Systems

### Scouting and Drafting

Your scheme determines your draft board. Two GMs can look at the same edge
rusher and see completely different values:

```
Edge Rusher Prospect — "Jordan Ellis"
  Speed: 92  Strength: 71  Pass Rush Moves: 85  Run Defense: 65

  3-4 OLB: ELITE prospect — speed rusher off the edge, scheme maximizes his
           pass rush while hiding his run defense
  4-3 DE:  GOOD prospect — useful pass rusher but may struggle setting the
           edge against the run
  3-4 DE:  POOR fit — not strong enough to two-gap at the point of attack
```

This means positional scarcity is scheme-dependent. A "weak" draft class for
one team might be deep for another.

### Free Agency

Players on the market have scheme histories. A corner who thrived in a Cover 3
system might struggle transitioning to man-heavy press coverage. This is a
factor in both the player's decision and your evaluation.

### Coaching Staff

Coaches have scheme specialties. Hiring decisions cascade:

- You hire an air raid offensive coordinator
- Your roster was built for power run
- Now you're facing a multi-year transition: draft speed players, release
  power players, or accept a mismatch and underperform while you retool
- Meanwhile, your existing players' development is disrupted — a power-blocking
  guard isn't suddenly going to become a pass-protection specialist

### Game Simulation

Scheme vs. scheme matchups influence outcomes. This isn't rock-paper-scissors —
talent still matters most — but scheme advantages create edges:

- A spread offense with four WRs against a base 4-3 that won't go to nickel
  creates mismatches
- A power run team in bad weather against a finesse defense has an edge
- A zone blitz can rattle a young QB but a veteran reads it and audibles
- Halftime adjustments from good coordinators can swing a game

### Scheme Transitions

Changing coaches means changing schemes, and changing schemes is costly:

- Players lose their scheme fit bonus during transition (1-2 season adjustment)
- Some players may never fit the new scheme and need to be moved
- Young players adapt faster than veterans
- Coaching staff continuity accelerates the transition
- The payoff is worth it if the new coaching staff better fits your long-term
  roster vision

This is why firing a coach is never just about the coach — it's about the
cascade. A new DC who runs a Cover 3 inherits a roster built for Cover 2.
Your $15M free safety you signed for his range is now a box safety in a
scheme that needs a different skill set. The coaching hire decision IS the
scheme decision, and both are roster decisions.

This creates one of the game's deepest strategic questions: do you hire
coaches who fit your current players, or draft players who fit the coaches
you want?
